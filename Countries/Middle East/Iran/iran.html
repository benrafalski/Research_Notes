Overview

Iran is a desert-mountainous country in southwest Asia. Their history dates back to the Achaemenid dynasty in 500 BE. Today, they have a theocratic Islamic regime marked by economic unrest. The capital of Iran is Tehran. Iran was previously known as Persia, which had a history of imperialism in the region. They have a strategic position with many natural resources, including petroleum. In the early 16th century Shii Islam began to grow in Iran. In 1925 the Pahlavi line ascended and attempted to modernize Iran but failed, leading to their overthrow in 1979 during the Iranian Revolution, which turned Iran into a Shii theocratic state. This left Iran in conflict with Iraq and in support of terrorism, which made them an international pariah. In the 1990s social unrest began to grow in opposition to the theocratic rule but was crushed in the early 2000s by the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps. [1]

Land

Borders
  • Neighboring Countries: Azerbaijan, Armenia, Turkmenistan, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Turkey, Iraq. [2]
  • Water: Caspian Sea, Persian Gulf, Gulf of Oman, Arabian Sea. [2]
Relief
  • Elevation: Most of the country is above 1,500 ft and one-sixth is over 6,500 ft. In the north near the Caspian Sea is about 90 ft. below sea level. [3]
  • Zagros Mountains: Part of the Alpine-Himalayan mountain range. Located in southwest Iran, run adjacent to the Persian Gulf, 900 miles long and 150 miles wide. Served as a natural barrier between the Ottomans and the Persians. Highest point is 14,000 ft. [3][4][5]
  • Elburz Mountains: Part of the Alpine-Himalayan mountain range. Run along the south shore of the Caspian sea, 560 miles long. Contains Irans highest mountain, Mount Damvand, at 18,600 ft. Tehran is just south of these mountains. They supply Tehran with water using the Safid Rud, Karaj, and Jajrud dams. [3][5][6][7]
  • Earthquakes: Iran has three volcanic regions. Violent earthquakes occur frequently. In 1990 an earthquake killed over 50,000 people. [3]
  • Kopet-Dag Range: Part of the Alpine-Himalayan mountain range. Located on the border of Turkmenistan for 400 miles. Highest point is 10,400 ft. [3][5][8]
  • Bashagard Range: Runs from Strait of Hormuz to Pakistan. [3]
  • Interior Plateau: Flat land in the center bounded by Zagros, Elburz, Kopet-Dag, and Bashagard mountains. It contains the Kavir desert in the north and the Lut desert in the southeast. [3][9][10]
Drainage
  • Strait of Hormuz: Links Persian Gulf to Gulf of Oman. About one-fifth of the worlds oil supply travels through here. [11][12]
  • Karun River: Large river in southwest Iran, starts in the Zagros and connects to the Shatt Al-Arab river in Iraq that empties into the Persian Gulf. Total length is 515 miles. [11][13]
  • Safid River: Starts in the Elburz mountains and runs to the Caspian Sea. Total length is 600 miles long. [11][14]
  • Zyandeh River: Starts in the Zagros to the Gv Khn Marsh in the middle of the country. [11]
  • Dams: Iran has the Dez dam, the Safid dam, Khrang dam, and Karun dam to provide hydroelectric power and water for irrigation. [11][15]
  • Lake Urmia: In northwest Iran near the border with Turkey. Largest in the Middle East at 2,000-2,300 square miles. [11][16]
  • Caspian Sea: Shores in Gilan, Mazandaran, and Golestan. Largest salt lake in the world. Water from Iranian rivers make up about 5% of the lake water. [11]
Climate
  • Wind Patterns: Generated from low pressure systems. Shamal wind blows in February to October northwest and the 120-day summer wind can reach 70 mph and is near Pakistan. Warm Arabian winds also come from the Persian Gulf. [17][18]
  • Temperatures: In the south, the average temperatures are around 110 F and in the north, the average temperatures are around 32 F. [17]
  • Precipitation: The south receives around 2 in and the area near the Caspian Sea gets about 78 in a year. The country average is 16 in per year. Over half of the rain occurs during the winter months. [17]
  • Seasons: The northwest has four distinct seasons while the southeast has summer and winter with short spring and falls. [17]
Life
  • Flora: One-tenth of Iran is forested, mostly in the Caspian region, with deciduous trees. The Zagros are covered in scrub oak forests. The deserts have sand dunes that support thickets of brush. [19]
  • Fauna: In the mountains you can find leopards, bears, hyenas, wild boars, ibex, gazelles and mouflons. In the center of the country you can find jackals and rabbits. In the Caspian region are cheetahs and pheasants. Seagulls, ducks, and geese live near the Caspian Sea and Persian Gulf. In the desert you can find gizzards. In the Elburz and Zagros are a wide range of toads, frogs, lizards, and other amphibians. There are 200 varieties of fish, shrimp, lobsters, and turtles. Sturgeon is a fish used to make caviar and is a major export from Iran. [19]
  • National Parks: Bakhtegan wildlife refuge, Turan protected area, Golestan national park. The hunting of swans, pheasants, deer, and other birds is illegal. [19]

People

Population
  • 87,529,000 in 2024
Ethnic Groups
  • By ethnicity: Persian (34%), Azerbaijani (16%), Kurd (13%), Lur (7%), Gilaki (5%), Mazadnerani (5%), Afghan (3%), and others (16%). [20]
  • By citizenship: Iranian (98%), Afghan (2%). [20]
  • Semites: There are about 2-3% Arabs in Iran who mainly reside in the Khuzestan province. There are also some Jews that are traced back to the Babylonian exile in the 6th century. [20]
  • Kurds: This minority has sought for an outright autonomous state and has refused to assimilate into the mainstream Iranian population. They are situated in the Zagros mountains in western Iran in the Kordistan province, where they are officially recognized by Iran. There is also Kurds in northeast Khorasan. [20][21]
  • Lur: Situated in Lorestan, Bakhtiari, and Kohglyeh va Byer Amad provinces in the Zagros mountains. They speak Luri and Laki, languages related to Persian. Nomadic tribes that rely on agriculture. [20,22]
  • Bakhtyari: Nomadic tribes related to the Lur. Speak Luri and are Shiite muslim. They live in the Zagros and rely on sheep, goats, and cattle for food. They have two main tribes, the Chahar Lang and the Haft Lang. The chiefs of these tribes are usually influential in Iranian politics and have held many public offices since the 1990s. Have notable good womens rights for Muslims. [20,23]
  • Baloch: Located in Balochistn that is in Iran and Pakistan. They speak Balochi. Divided into two groups: the Sulaimani and the Makrani. Consists of several nomadic tribes, each run by a chief. Their religion is Sunni Islam. Only 20% of them live in Iran. [20,24]
  • Turkic: Main Turkic group is Azerbaijanis. Farming and herding population in northwestern Iran. Other groups include the Qashqai and Shiraz in the north of the Persian Gulf, and the Turkmen in Khorasan. [20]
Languages
  • Persian (Farsi): This is the official language of Iran. More than half of the population speaks Persian. Is the predominant language for literature, journalism, and sciences. Is the most common language used for internet blogs in the middle east, despite attempts by the Islamic regime at internet censorship. [25,26]
  • Kurdish: Spoken by less than one-tenth of the population. Is an Iranian language related to Persian. [25,27]
  • Luri: Spoken by the Lur and Bakhtyari. [25]
  • Azerbaijani: Most popular Turkic language. One-fourth of the population speaks Turkic. [25]
  • Arabic: Spoken by a small number of the native population. Many parts of Persian come from Arabic. This is also the main religious language since the Quran was written in Arabic. [25]
  • English and French: Not used very commonly but taught in schools and universities. [25,26]
Religion
  • Twelver Shii Muslim: Over 99% of the country is Muslim, of these 95% are Twelver Shii Muslims. Twelver Shii is the official state religion and has been since 1501. Twelver Shi'a is currently the worlds largest Shii group, with 40% of the Shii population residing in Iran. The Twelver clergy has been the main political force in Iran since 1979. The priests in Twelver are a community of scholars called ulama. To become part of the ulama you must be a male Muslim who attended an Islamic college. Lower clergy members are called mullahs and staff a majority of local religious sites. Authorities in Islamic law are called mujtahids and the single most honored mujtahid becomes ayatollah and is called marja. There is no religious hierarchy so believers can only avoid sin by following the teaching of their chosen marja. This has given more power to the ulama in recent years. [28,29]
  • Sunni Muslim: About 7% of the population is Sunni. Mostly made up of Kurds and Turkmen. [28]
  • Minorities: Most significant are the Christians, Jews, and Zoroastrians. Zoroastrians are an ancient Iranian religion and they are in Yazd, Kerman, and Tehran. Under the Abbasids the jizya tax was created and forced many Zoroastrians to convert, the British finally forced the Qajar dynasty to eliminate the jizya and under the Pahlavi dynasty Zoroastrians were treated better but this was all reverted after the revolution. Ever since the 1979 revolution the minorities have not been treated equally even though they are recognized in the Iranian constitution. The Bahi Faith has especially been persecuted by the Muslim clergy and Iranian government, especially after the revolution when 300,000 of them were persecuted by the government. Bahai religion originated in Shiraz. [28,30,31,32]
Settlement Patterns
  • Very High density provinces: Tehran, Alborz. [33]
  • High density provinces: Hamadan, Gilan, Mazandaran. [33]
  • Medium-high density provinces: Golestan, Azarbayjan, Ardabil, Kermanshah, Qazvin, Qom, Khuzestan. [33]
  • Medium density provinces: Kordestan, Zanjan, Markazi, Korestan, Bakhtiari. [33]
  • Medium-low density provinces: Khorasan, Esfahan, Fars, Bushehr, Kohgiluyeh va Bowyer Ahmad, Ilman. [33]
  • Low population density: Kerman, Yazd, Hormozgan, Baluchestan, Semnan. [33]
  • Urbanization: 75% of Irans population is urban. Tehran and Esfahan are the two most populous cities. Central and east Iran are sparsely populated due to the desert climate. [33]
Population Trends
  • Age breakdown: 15-29 (30%), 30-44 (24%), under 15 (24%), 45-59 (14%), 60-74 (6%), 75+ (2%).[34]
  • Life expectancy: 73 years for men and 76 years for women.[34]
  • Migration trends: Since the 1960s there has been increased internal migration to major cities in Iran. After the 1979 revolution there was a major outflow of educated Iranians to the west, especially in California and other parts of the US. In 1979 many Jews and Bahais also fled in fear of persecution.[34]

Economy

Overview
  • Overview: $359.7 billion, $4,091.21 per capita. [35]
  • International isolation: Due to Irans xenophobia from its conservative politicians and sanctions from the US and others they have been isolated on international markets, hampering their economic growth and restricted foreign investment. In 1996, the US expanded sanctions on a petroleum embargo, technology exports, and a variety of Iranian imports. [35]
  • Economic goals: Since 1979, Iran has aimed to become economically independent, full of unemployment, and a high standard of living. [35]
  • Economic challenges: Rapidly increasing population, which has increasingly become younger. Failure of agriculture production, forcing migration into Irans largest cities. High unemployment and inflation. Reliance on petroleum exports. [35]
  • State planning: Public sector includes major industries, banks, insurance, utilities, communication, foreign trade, and transportation. Production and distribution of goods and services are run using cooperatives. The private sector fills in the rest. Initially after 1979, the leftists and conservatives in government debated the expansion of privatization in the economy. The Iran-Iraq war prompted state intervention and the government gained a monopoly on all income-producing activities after they nationalized all banks and insurance companies. [35]
  • Islamic Law: Charging interest on loans and certain financial speculation is illegal. [35]
  • Reform: Iran president Mohammad Khatami called for economic reforms in 1997 for more privatization. However, nothing so far has been done to take steps towards this. Reformists have also refused to allow foreign investment into the country, such as from the US, as well. [35]
Agriculture
  • Agriculture: One-third of Irans land is farmland but only one-tenth is under cultivation because of soil and water issues, mostly in the west and northwest. One-third of the cultivated land is irrigated, the rest is dry farmed. Agriculture accounts for about one-fifth of Irans GDP and jobs. Most farms are small and not economically viable, which is why there has been mass migration to cities. This has led to low crop yield and poverty in rural areas. After 1979, many farmers claimed their land privately. In the 1990s, large government infusions improved farming production marginally. Iran farms cereals, fruits, vegetables, cotton, sugar, nuts, olives, spices, tea, tobacco, and herbs. Almost all Saffron also comes from Iran. [36,37]
  • Forestry: About one-tenth of Iran is forests, mainly around the Caspian Sea. They produce plywood, fibreboard, and lumber. [36]
  • Fishing: Iran mainly fishes in the Caspian for sturgeon, bream, whitefish, salmon, mullet, carp, catfish, perch, and roach. In the Persian Gulf they catch shrimp and prawn. Iran uses fish domestically and exports them. [36]
  • Livestock: Iran mainly raises sheep, followed by goats then cattle. Poultry is also widespread for eggs and meat and camels are used for transportation. [36]
Resources
  • Petroleum: Petroleum is the most valuable resource for Iran, they produce 4.76% of the worlds petro. The government owned National Iranian Oil Company (NIOC) produces oil domestically and for exporting. Iran has a pipeline that goes to Khrk island, where it is packed onto a tanker shipped around the world. Irans main refining facility is in Abadan, which was rebuilt after the Iraq war. The NIOC also has refineries at Esfahan, Shiraz, Lavan Island, Tehran, and Tabriz. These sites produce fuels for heating and the transportation industry. Iran has four supergiant oil fields including Ahvaz, Marun, Agha Jari, and Gach Saran. [38,39]
  • Natural gas: Iran has one-tenth of the worlds total natural gas. Gas fields can be found in the Elburz, in Khorasan, in the Persian Gulf, in the Caspian Sea, and in the South Pars region. Distribution lines run to Tehran, Kashan, Esfahan, Shiraz, Mashhad, Ahvaz, and Alborz. Iran has two state owned trunk lines that distribute to Russia, eastern Europe, Pakistan, Turkey, and India. [38]
  • Petrochemical: Mainly situated in the south and was rebuilt after the Iraq war. The Razi Petrochemical Company is a subsidiary of the National Petrochemical Company of Iran and produces ammonia, phosphates, sulfur, liquid gas, and light oil. [38]
  • Mining: Iran mines for coal in Khorasan, Kerman, Semnan, Mazandaran, Gilan, Tehran, Azarbayjan, and Esfahan. Copper mining is done mainly in Kerman. In the 1990s they started mining uranium and gold for profit. Other deposits include lead, zinc, fireclay, chalk, lime, gypsum, ochre, and kaolin. [38]
  • Power: Most of the countrys electricity comes from coal, petroleum, and natural gas. They also have many dams that provide hydroelectric power. [38]
  • Nuclear: In 1973, the Atomic Energy Organization (AEO) was established to make 20 nuclear power plants. In 1978 two reactors were near completion. Only one was finished with the help of Russia in 2011. In 2002, it was discovered Iran had uranium enrichment facilities and many feared they were trying to make nuclear weapons. Iran says they only want it for peaceful purposes but they have been punished with sanctions since 2006. [38]
Industry
  • Manufacturing: Tehran is the main hub for manufacturing. Manufactured products are distributed by air, truck, rail, camel, mule, and donkey. Iran began industrializing in the 1950s and now produces automobiles, electric appliances, telecommunications equipment, industrial machinery, paper, rubber products, steel, food products, wood and leather products, textiles, and pharmaceuticals. [40]
  • Textiles: Textile mills are mainly situated in Esfahan and in the Caspian region. Iran is world famous for their hand woven carpets, which makes up a large part of their exports and rural economies. [40]
  • Construction: From 1950-1970 Iran saw a construction boom due to mass oil/gas profits and easy credit. In the 1970s Iran was hit with rapid inflation and the construction bubble popped when rates were raised. By the 1980s the industry somewhat revived but housing shortages in urban areas are still prevalent today. [40]
Finance
  • Credit: The government controls loans and credit through banks. In 1979 they nationalized all the banks and made the Islamic Bank of Iran. Since banks cannot charge interest they either have service charges or commission. Iran has 10 banks in total that are either commercial, industrial, or agricultural. [41]
  • National currency: Rial, 1 USD is 42312 rail. Issued by the Central Bank of the Islamic Republic of Iran. [41]
Trade
  • Imports: Although Iran aims to be self-sufficient they still heavily rely on imports. They mainly import foodstuffs, followed by manufacturing equipment. Their major import sources are UAE (31%), China (24%), Turkey (10%), Germany (4%), and Russia (3%). [42]
  • Exports: Petroleum exports make up a majority of Irans export profits. They also export carpets, nuts, fruits, chemicals, and metals. They mainly export to China (27%), Iraq (12%), Turkey (8%), UAE (7%), and Afghanistan (2%). [42]
Services
  • Services: The service sector makes up two-fifths of GDP in Iran. A majority of this is from government spending on the military, government salaries, and social services. [43]
  • Tourism: Between 1964-78 the tourism sector began to grow due to the construction of highways and hotels. After the 1979 revolution, the tourism sector collapsed since the Islamic regime discouraged western travelers. As a result, tourism is a small part of the economy today. [43]
Labor
  • Unions: Workers have a right to unionize in theory but there is no actual union system in Iran. They are supposed to be represented by the state-sponsored Workers House. Worker strikes are not respected by the state and usually met with police retaliation. [44]
  • Workforce: Manufacturing/construction (25%), agriculture (20%), services (18%), transportation/communication (18%), finance (18%). [44]
  • Women and refugees: Women are allowed to work outside the home but have restrictions on the number of jobs they can have. Women make up a very small portion of the overall workforce. Some of Irans refugees are allowed to work, most of which do low-paying construction and agriculture. [44]
  • Worker Laws: The minimum age to work is 15 years old but many sectors are exempt from following this law. The work week is 6 days with Fridays off. [44]
  • Taxation: Most of the governments revenue comes from petroleum and natural gas profits. Iran used corporation taxes and import tariffs as well. Voluntary Islamic taxes are also collected from Muslims including: khums, zakat, and kharaj. Khums is an income tax that is one-fifth of income and is part of Twelver Islam only. Zakat is levied on property like food, cattle, gold/silver, and goods and is collected by the ulama to be distributed to the poor and needy. Kharaj is a land tax equal to one tenth of their produce, under Islamic law only Muslims can own land. [44,45,46]
Transport/
Telecoms
  • Roads: Buses and trucks are the major transportation method for people and goods. Since the 1990s the government has focused heavily on infrastructure, having already paved half of the road in Iran today. [47]
  • Railways: The main rail runs between the Caspian Sea and the Persian Gulf. In 1971 it was linked to Turkey and the EU system which helped with transportation and trade. In 1971 it was also linked to Singapore. In 1991 the railway was linked to Armenia and Azerbaijan. The rail is also linked to central Asia in the northeast city of Bafq. [47]
  • Water travel: The Karun is the only navigable river and is used for cargo and passenger travel. Lake Urmia is also used for cargo and passenger ferry travel. [47]
  • Ports: Iran has five major ports in the Persian Gulf: the largest is Bandar Abbas, Khorramshahr and Bandar-e Khomeyni are used for oil, and Bushehr and Chah Bahar. There are also two Caspian ports: Bandar-a Anzali and Bandar-e Torkaman. [47]
  • Air travel: Iran Air is their state-owned airline. There are also EU, Asian, and African airlines in Iran. Tehran, Abadan, Esfahan, Shiraz, and Bandar Abbas have international airports. [47]
  • Telecommunications: Telecoms media is mainly state-owned. Since the 1990s the government has infused lots into telecoms infrastructure. By 2000 almost all Iranaians had access to telephone services. Cell phones and the internet have also allowed Iranains to view the outside world and global culture. [47]

Government

Constitution
  • Constitutional Framework: The 1979 constitution declared a unitary Islamic republic with one legislative house. The executive, parliamentary, and judiciary branches are all overseen by oversight institutions run by the Islamic clergy. The head-of-state and head of the oversight institutions is called the rahbar. [48]
  • Velayat-e faqih: Started by Ayatollah Khomeini after the revolution. Gives political leadership to the faqih (Islamic jurist), which was him at the time. [48]
  • Assembly of Experts: 83 members that are from the ulama. They chose a leader among the Islamic clergy. Can remove the rahbar from office if they see fit. Originally formed to draft the constitution. [48]
  • Rahbar: Supreme leader of Iran. Appoints leaders for the IRGC, members of the Council of Guardians, and members of the judiciary. Responsible for declaring war and is commander-in-chief. Sets the political policy for Iran. No term limits. First was Khomeini, second was Ali Khamenei. [48]
  • President: Elected by universal suffrage. Head of the executive branch. Must be a native Iranian Shii. Didnt have power until 1989 when an amendment removed the prime minister as a position. Responsible for selecting the Council of Ministers for approval, appoints some members of the Committee to Determine the Expediency of the Islamic Order, and is the chairman of the Supreme Council for National Security. [48]
  • Majles (Islamic Consultative Assembly): 290 member (deputy) unicameral legislature. Universal suffrage elections every four years. Enact all legislation and can impeach the president with two-thirds vote. [48]
  • Council of Guardians: De facto upper legislative house. 12 members of jurists. 6 are Islamic law experts appointed by the Rahbar and 6 are civil law experts nominated by the Supreme Judicial Council and approved by the Majles. All legislation passed by the Majles must also be passed by the guardians based on constitutionality and Islamic law standards. All candidates seeking election must also be approved by the guardians. [48,49]
  • Committee to Determine the Expediency of the Islamic Order: Some members from the Council of Guardians and some appointed by the president. Made in 1988 to sort out disagreements between the Guardians and Majles. [48]
Local Government
  • States: Iran is divided into provinces (ostanha), provinces are divided into counties (shahrestanha), counties are divided into districts (bakhshha), districts are divided into towns (dehestanha). [50]
  • Leadership: Minister of interior appoints governor-generals for provinces, governors for counties, and mayors for cities. Each province, county and district have councils and a Supreme Council of Provinces is formed from province councils. Each city also has elected councilmen. Villages are administered by a local village master. [50]
Justice
  • Judiciary: Supreme Court, Supreme Judicial Council, and lower courts. Chief justice and prosecutor general must be mujtahid. All judicial decisions are made according to sharia Islamic law according to the constitution. [51]
  • Law code: All laws conform to sharia law. Includes retributions such as if someone is murdered the victims family can execute the killer. State executions are standard for many crimes like adultery, drinking alcohol, and homosexuality. In 2022, they carried out 576 executions, 255 of the executions were drug-related (which is against international law), 5 executions were for people below 18 years old. The Special Clerical Court is a separate court to try clergy members. [51,52]
Political Process
  • Elections: Every four years. Overseen by the Council of Guardians. Universal suffrage for 18 years and older. [53]
  • Political parties: Political parties have a small role in the Iranian government. After the revolution the Islamic Republic Party became the ruling party. The Muslin Peoples Republic Party was the largest party that was critical of Khomeinis reforms after the revolution. However, in the 1980s Khomeini ordered to dissolve both of these parties and outlawed the opposition parties: Tudeh, Mojahedin-e Khalq, and Democratic Party of Iranian Kurdistan. [53]
  • Ideology: With no strong parties elections focus on ideology including: principlists, reformist, or moderates. Principlists are conservative Islamists, reformists are liberal and want to increase international relations, and moderates are anyone who is in between these two camps. [53]
Security
  • Military: Iran had a strong military before 1979 but after the revolution it collapsed. From 1980-88 Iran had a war with Iraq and their military has been strong ever since. All males are required to serve for 21 months in the military. The army is the largest branch and their air force and navy are small. [54]
  • Weapons of mass destruction: In the 1980s Iran began WMD programs including nuclear, biological, and chemical weapons. By the 1990s they had successfully begun producing medium/intermediate range missiles. [54]
  • Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC): Branch of the military separate from the normal army and is Irans main military force. Established during the 1979 revolution by Khomeini to safeguard the Islamic republic by organizing all the Islamic paramilitary forces together. They have their own army, navy, air force, and intelligence agency. In 1997 the IRGC began to become more politically active and in the early 2000s they gained large political and economic power. [54,55]
  • Quds Force: A secret wing of the IRGC that is responsible for foreign operations. The Quds Force has supported Hezbollah in Lebanon, the Northern Alliance in Afghanistan, the Badr Organization in Iraq against the US, Bashar al-Assad in the Syrian civil war, the Houthis in Yemen, ground forces against ISIS, and Hamas in Gaza. [54,56]
  • Law Enforcement: Law Enforcement Command is the police force. The Guidance Patrol is the special morality police force who enforce things like hijab coverage and not allowing people to have sex. Murder rate is 2.6, 228 prisoners per capita, 7.1/10 on the global crime index (6th internationally). [54,57,58]
Health and Welfare
  • Health: Got better after WWII. Has had two Cholera outbreaks in the late 20th century. Shortage of doctors, nurses, and medical supplies. Public hospitals provide free treatment for poor people. There is private care as well but even with this treatment overall is inadequate. The Ministry of Health, Treatment, and Medical Education is in charge of health services. [61]
  • Welfare: The Ministry of State for Welfare, Foundation of the Oppressed, and the Martyr Foundation (for families and war casualties) are in charge of welfare. [61]
Housing
  • Housing Crisis: Migration to urban cities after the revolution created a housing crisis that the government began addressing in the 1990s. The government has mostly provided government credit for private sector development in large cities, mainly Tehran. [62]
  • Living Conditions: Major cities have purified water in homes while in rural areas they use wells. Central heating in buildings is extremely rare. The urban poor and refugee population experience extremely harsh living conditions. [62]
Education
  • Lower education: Primary education is mandatory for ages 6-11. 80% of men and 66% of women are literate. After primary school students attend a three year program to determine if they will go to an academic, scientific, or vocational program in high school. Public schools and primary/secondary education is controlled by the Ministry of Education and Training, which has led to some administrative weakness. [63,64]
  • Religious education: All schools and universities must promote Islam. Universities consider Islamic values in acceptance for both students and faculty. [63]
  • Universities: 57% of the population goes to post secondary schooling. Entry into college is administered through competitive national exam. Main college is the University of Tehran. After the revolution the higher education system somewhat collapsed due to professors fleeing the country and a shortage of skilled teachers is still prevalent. This has led to lower enrollment counts and students to study abroad. However, by 2000 over half of the students were women. Universities are controlled by the Ministry of Higher Education and Culture, and medical schools are under the Ministry of Health, Treatment, and Medical Education. Top university is Tehran University at 346 in the world. [63,65,91]

Culture

Culture
  • Cultural milieu: Iran has a rich cultural history dating back about 2,500 years. Their identity is one of the oldest in the world and has contributed to world culture enormously. Iranians believe that Iran will once again rise, as they have time and time again in history. Iranian leaders find it laughable that a country as young as the US is arrogant enough to lecture them at all. Daily life in Iran is heavily influenced by Shii Islam but art, literature, and architecture are all related to their historic Persian culture. [67,68]
  • Multiculturalism: Persian culture is the predominant culture of Iran but Armenian, Azerbaijani, and Kurdish ethnic groups also maintain strong ties to their cultural groups. [67]
Society
  • Martyrdom: Shii martyr culture originated in 680 when the third imam, al-Husayn, was killed at the Battle of Karbala attempting to return himself to power. Since Shii are a minority in Islam they have faced persecution and have seen themselves as martyrs attempting to return al-Husayn to power. Each year on the anniversary of al-Husayns death the Shii mourn on the holiday of Ashura. During Ashura, Shii believers perform rituals of self-beatings with their bare-hands or with chains and blades sometimes. Most religious events reference Karbala and each month at least one day of mourning is observed. [69,70]
  • Eids: Celebrate two Islamic festivals: Eid al-Adha and Eid al-Fitr. Eid al-Fitr comes first and marks the end of Ramadan (Muslim holy month of fasting) where Muslims visit family and friends, exchange gifts, wear new clothes, and visit the graves of relatives. Eid al-Adha comes after and marks the end of hajj (pilgrimage to Mecca), three day holiday where families sacrifice an acceptable animal (sheep, goat, camel, cow) and divide the flesh among themselves and exchange gifts. [69,71,72]
  • Iranian calendar and Nowruz: The current Iranian calendar was adopted under Reza Shah in 1925. It is a solar calendar with Persian names for months and the first year starts at Hijra (when Muhammad traveled from Mecca to Medina in 622) or is of the Muslim era (as opposed to the Christian era). The new year is on March 20-21 depending on the year and is called Nowruz. Followed by a week-long holiday and ends with a day of picnicking in the countryside. In 2023 it was 1401 in Iran. [69,73,74,75,76]
  • Birthday of the 12th imam: Celebrated with lights and decorations. Twelver Shia expect the 12th imams second coming at the end of days and believe that he (Muhammad al-Mahdi) is the final of the imams. [69,]
  • Cuisine: Largely influenced by Arab culture. Rice and lamb play a part in almost every meal. Onions are also a staple in Iranian dishes. Milk, cheese, and yogurt are common in Persian dishes. Preparation styles are simple, like kabobs. Common flavoring includes: saffron, lime, mint, turmeric, rosewater, pomegranates, and walnuts. [69,77]
Arts
  • Crafts: Carpets are prevalent throughout the country with design patterns taking the name of the province they are from. The hand-woven industry has managed to survive textile competition and carpets are used domestically and exported. Metallic crafts are made using gold, silver, copper, and natural pearls. Wooden crafts are also made for domestic and export use. Tile mosaics are manufactured in Tehran. Iranians also use stone and clay to make utensils, trays, dishes, and vases. [78]
  • Architecture: Chogha Zanbil and Persepolis are two ancient sites that are on the UNESCO World Heritage List. Chogha Zanbil is an ancient temple palace in Khuzestan and Persepolis is an ancient city in Fars. There are also three Armenian Christian sites that are World Heritage sites. Neyshabur, Esfahan, and Shiraz also have lots of Islamic mosques, madrasahs (muslim schools), shrines, and palaces. Western architecture techniques began to trend under the monarchy but in 1967 the National Council for Iranian Architecture was formed and discouraged western styles in favor of Iranian styles. Under the monarchy the Shahyad tower, renamed the Azadi tower, was completed in Tehran. [78,79,80,81]
  • Visual Arts: Does not have a visual arts culture. Only exceptions are miniature paintings such as Jalayirid, Shiraz, and Esfahan schools but these died out in the 18th century. After the revolution visual arts were restricted but have continued to develop with the internet. [78]
  • Music: In the 20th century a music conservatory was founded in Tehran. However, after the revolution music was banned. Western pop is still fashionable among the youth and Iranian pop groups sometimes perform but are subject to punishment. Irans most famous singer is Googoosh, she was silenced from the revolution until 2000 by Iranian authorities. [78,82]
  • Literature: Iran is best known for their literature. Famous Persian authors included Ferdowsi, Nezami, Hafez, Jami, and Rumi. However, publication of classical works is restricted by the Islamic clergy. Iranian literature was influenced by the west in the 19/20th centuries and served as a form of protest with authors such as Sadeq Hedayat, Jalal Al-e Ahmad, Sadeq-e Chubak, Ahmad Shamlu, and Forough Forrokhazad. After the revolution, many authors went into exile and began to publish abroad. After the revolution a feminist movement also formed with writers such as Shahrnoush Parsipour and Moniru Ravanipur. [78]
  • Cinema: Movies are the most popular form of entertainment in Iran. After the revolution films were banned but then the government began subsidizing them if they promoted Islam but they were not very popular. Later, films about the Iran-Iraq war gained popularity. In the 1990s the public wanted better relations with the west and government moderation and this is when the Iranian film industry became one of the most prestigious in the world, winning numerous international awards. Directors include: Bahram Bayzai, Abbas Kiarostami, Mohsen Makhmalbaf, Dariyush Mehrjui, Jafar Panahi, and Asghar Farhadi. Women films also gained traction with directors such as Rakhshan Bani Etemad and Tahmineh Milani. [78,83,84,85]
Institutions
  • Museums: Few museums other than the Golestn Palace Museum in Tehrn, the All Saviours Cathedral Museum of Jolf in Esfahan, and the Tehrn Museum of Modern Art. [86]
  • Academic organizations: The Ancient Iranian Cultural Society, the Iranian Mathematical Society, and the Iranian Society of Microbiology are all in Tehran. [86]
Sports
  • Under monarch: Traditional sports are wrestling, horse racing, and bodybuilding. Western sports rugby and volleyball are also popular. Most popular game is soccer and their team was really good before the revolution. Before the revolution Iran also competed in the Olympics. Polo originated in Persia and was played by the kings of Iran. After the revolution it somewhat fell out of favor but is making a slight comeback in recent years. [87,88,89]
  • Post-revolution: In 1979, sports were seen as a rival to the mosque, teams were nationalized, and women were prevented from playing. Since the 1990s soccer has made somewhat of a comeback with the desire for liberalization. [87]
Media
  • Newspapers: Primarily based in Tehran. Cannot publish anything that is anti-Muslim. Foreign journalists are rarely allowed into the country. The constitution has freedom of the press but censorship is rampant in journalism and on the internet, journalism has still contributed greatly to political reforms since the 1990s though. Two biggest newspapers are Ettela'at and Kayhan. [90]
  • Radio/TV: Controlled by the government and reaching the entire country. Personal satellites are illegal but are not strongly enforced. [90]
