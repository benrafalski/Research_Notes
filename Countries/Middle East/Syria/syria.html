Overview

Located in southwestern Asia. Territory included Golan Heights that is occupied by Israel since the 1967 six day war. The capital is Damascus. They gained independence in 1946. Ever since then, life has been marked by social, religious, and political unrest. In 1970, authoritarian ruler Pres. Hafez al-Assad came to power and focused on military power to take back the territory they lost to Israel in 1967. In 2000, Hafaez died and Bashar al-Assad took over. He would continue his father's authoritarian style of rule leading to the outbreak of the Syrian civil war in 2011. [1]

Land

Borders
  • Neighboring Countries: Turkey, Iraq, Jordan, Lebanon, and Israel. [2]
  • Water: Mediterranean Sea. [2]
Relief
  • Elevation: [2]
  • Al-Ansariyyah Mountains: Mountains just off the coast of the Mediterranean. Run north to south and has a highest point of 5,125 feet. [2]
  • Anti-Lebanon Mountains: Sparsely populated and run along the border of Syria-Lebanon. Contains Syria's highest peak at 9,232ft called Mount Hermon. Part of Mount Hermon is in the Golan Heights and Israel has developed these slopes for recreation such as skiiing. [2,3,4]
  • Mount al-Duruz: Mount of the Druze, located in southern Syria. Is 5,900ft high. [2,5]
  • Syrian Desert: Runs from southern Syria into northern Saudi Arabia and from the Jordan river to the middle of Iraq. Arid rock-desert wasteland about 1,000ft above sea level that gets less than 5in of water a year. Has some oil exploration. [2,6]
Drainage
  • Euphrates River: Main water source and only navigable river. 1,740 miles long. Runs from Turkey, through the middle of Syria, and through Iraq near to the Persian Gulf. Euphrates dam is in al-Raqqah and was completed in 1973 and also has a power plant. [7,8,9]
  • Lake Al-Asad: Revervoir behind the Euphrates dam on the border of Halab and al-Raqqah. Began to fill in 1973. [7]
  • Orontes River: Flows west of the Anti-Lebanon mountains over the northern Lebanon border, running east of the Al-Ansariyyah mountains and into Turkey where it drains into the Mediterranean. Main river in the mountains. [7,10]
  • Lake Qattineh: Dam on the Orontes forms the lake locted in Hims near the Lebanon border. [10]
  • Yarmuk River: Stems from the northern tip of the Jordan river and forms the boundary between Jordan and Syria along the Golan Heights. About 20 miles long. [7,11]
  • Al-Jabbul Lake: Smaller lake southeast of Aleppo. [7]
  • Barada River: Flows from the Anti-Lenanon mountains through Damascus. 52 miles long. [7,33]
  • Smaller lakes: Jayrud northeast of Damascus, Khatuniyyah northeast of al-Hasakah, Muzayrib in Dara, and Qattinah in Homs. [7]
Climate
  • Wind patterns: Blows east, north, and west in winter. Blows north and west in Summer. In the summer there are khamsin, hot/dry/dusty winds that blow S/SE and reach temps of 104F and raise a dust wall 5,000ft high. [13,14]
  • Temperatures: On coast summers at around 80F and winters at 50F. Inner Syria has dry climate with hot summers at 90-110F and cold winters at 35-40F. [12]
  • Precipitation: Coast and western Mountains get 30-40in per year. Inland is about 5-20in annually. [12]
  • Seasons: Coast has Mediterranean climate with dry season from May to October. Inland it will sometimes snow in winter. [12]
Life
  • Flora: Yew, lime, fir trees and date palms. Grains, olive trees, grapevines, apricot trees, oaks, and poplars, lemon/orange trees. Garigure and maquis scrubs. Some forests found in the mountains and can have oil-rich fruit, laurel, sumac, pistachio, mulberry, khubbayzah, akkub, truffles, and licorice. [15]
  • Fauna: Wolves, hyenas, foxes, badgers, wild boar, jackals, deer, bears, squirrles, gazelles, jerboas, vipers, lizards, chameleons, eagles, buzzards, kites, falcons, mosquitoes, sandflies, grasshoppers, and locusts. For transportation and travel, mules are used in the mountains and camels in the steppe. Horses, donkeys, cattle, sheep, goats, and chickens are also domesticates. Bee colonies are also kept. [15]

People

Population
  • 23,434,000 in 2024
Ethnic Groups
  • By ethnicity: Syrian Arab (75%), Bedouin Arab (7%), Kurd (7%), Palestinian (4%), Armenian (3%). [16]
  • Arabia/Mesopotamian influence: Aramaeans, Assyrians, Chaldeans, and Canaanites procduced the dominant Arab culture. Aramaeans were in Aram in northern Syria from 1100-720 BC. Assyrians were a great kingdom and ruled northern Mesopotamia (northern Iraq to southeast Turkey) from 1300-600 BC. Chaldeans were in southern Iraq to northern Syria from 625-539 BCE. Canaanites in the area of Syria and Palestine, land of histroical Israel. [16,17,18,19,20]
  • Arab: Speak Arabic, mostly Muslim, and are the majority in Syria. Majority live in small/isolated farming villages and follow traditional values. [16,24]
  • Bedouin: Arabic speaking people. Animal herders that migrate to the desert in the rainy season. Camel, sheep, and goat Bedouin nomads can be found in Syria. Tribal, patriarchal, and polygymous. Head of tribes and familites are called sheikh. Have integrated into governments and society after WWI. After 1950, they were pressured to abandon nomadism and have begun to use cars. [16,21]
  • Kurdish: Found in northeastern Syria and mostly speak Arabic. In 1962, the Syrian government took citizenship away from all Kurdish people in Al-Hashakah if they couldn't prove they lived in Syria since 1945, which limited their rights such as voting, traveling (passports), owning property, job discrimination, and rejection from public services. Syrian-Kurdish marriages were also not recognized. In 1978, the Kurdish Workers' Party (PKK) was formed in Turkey. In the 2010s they grew in northeastern Syria during the civil war and fought heavily against the growing ISIS. In 2016, Turkey invaded northeastern Syria on the PKK. In the 2020s fighting between Turkey and the PKK continues to spill into Syria. [16]
  • Armenian: Two groups, early settlers that are Arabized and later immigrants who came after WWI and still have their culture/language. In 1915, the young Turks of the Ottoman government carried out the Armenian genocide and deported them all from easter Anatolia (Turkey) to Syria/Mesopotamia. They decended from Indo-Europeans, speak Armenian, and mostly belong to the Armenian Apostolic Church. [16,23]
  • Assyrians: Immigrated from Iraq in the 1930s and have quickly assimilated due to intermarriage and migration to cities. Speak Syriac. Have been vicitms of mass violence and displacement in the 2000s. Many have left to the west, Turkey, Jordan, or Lebanon. [16,25]
  • Turkmen: Integrated with Arabs and Kurds and have kept their identity in northern villages. [16]
Languages
  • Arabic: Spoken by the majority of the population. [16]
  • Kurdish: Spoken by Kurds in northeast/northwest. [16]
  • Armenian: Spoken in Aleppo and other cities. [16]
  • Turkish Spoken along the border with Turkey. [16]
  • Other: Adyghian, a Russian language, is spoken by a minoroty. English and French among the educated. [16,26]
Religion
  • Sunni Muslim: 74% of Syria is Sunni. Are the majority everywhere except Al-Sawayda and Latakia. Regard themselves as the mainstream branch of Islam over the Shi'i. [27,30]
  • Alawite Muslim: 11% of Syria is Alawite. Concentrated in Latakia, Homs, and Hamah. Alawites are any sect of Shi'i Muslims living in Syria. Consider themselves modern Shi'ites, similar to Twelvers. [27,28]
  • Druze: 5% of Syria is Druze. Concentrated in Al-Sawayda, Damascus, Allepo, and Qunaytirah. About 600,000 of them in Syria. Most came in 1700s from Labanon to Mount Druze. Led Syria's first national uprising against the French in 1925. They have remained politically active after that. [27,29]
  • Christians: 10% of Syria is Christian. Greek Orthodox, Greek Catholic, Syrian Orthodox, Armenian Catholic, Armenian Apostolic, Syrian Catholic, Maronite, Protestant, Nestorian, Latin, and Chaldean.[27]
  • Jewish: Only four remain in Syria. Used to be thousands but most Jews left to New York and Israel after 1973. [27,31]
Settlement Patterns
  • Very high population dense govenorates: Latakia, Tartus, Damascus. [32]
  • High population dense govenorates: Aleppo, Idlib, Rif Dimashq, Dara [32]
  • Medium population dense govenorates: Hama, Al-Hasakah, As-Suwayda [32]
  • Low population dense govenorates: Raqqa, Homs, Dier ez-Zor, Quneitra. [32]
  • Rural Settlement: 45% of Syria is rural. Rural villages are close and have narrow streets. Usually a small central common with a minaret for prayer. Also have shops with items from cities. Villages are agricultral and market oriented. Due to government subsidies, they have been revived since the decline of the 1958-61 drought. [32]
  • Urban Settlement: 55% of Syria is urban. Islamic tradition is followed in urban cities like Damascus and Aleppo. Damascus serves as main center of education, culture, industry, marketing, public utilities, and transport facitilites. Aleppo is the largest city and is a trade center. Lakakia is a port and also is a tourist center. [32]
Population Trends
  • Age breakdown: Population is very young. Under 15 (33%), 15-29 (27%), 30-44 (21%), 45-59 (12%), 60+ (7%) [34]
  • Life expectancy: 69 years for men and 75 years for women. [34]
  • Migration trends: After 1967 much of the population from the Golan Heights was expelled to other parts of Syria and are still displaced today. After 1948, 80,000 Pals also came to Syria and have grown to 400,000 today. In 2003, after the Iraq war, over 1 million Iraqis fled to Syria. Due to the process of socialist transformation under the Baath Party and al-Assad, the wealthy and religious minorities have fled the country. Since the 1950s there has been a brain drain from Syria to other Arab states and the west. [34]

Economy

Overview
  • Overview: $11.08 billion, $533.39 per capita. [35]
  • Socialism: In 1963, socialism became Syrian official economic policy with socialist transformation and industrialization following. Syrian foreign trade is state controlled. The government owns oil refineries, electrical plants, railways, and many manufacturing plants.[35]
  • Private industry: Retail trade is still in the private sector with small private businesses existing. Deposit rates are also high to encourage private savings. The government also guarentees investments by citizens of other Arab states. In 2001 private banking was legalized and in 2000 state owned land was privatized due to corruption on state farms. However, the Assad regime has been hesitant to liberalize the economy due to fear of political instability. [35,37,38]
  • Sanctions: The EU has banned the export of luxury good to Syria to pressure al-Assad and his family following the breakout of the civil war in 2011. [35,36]
Agriculture
  • Agriculture: Makes up about 25% of the workforce, mostly townspeople. Wheat and sugar beats are the two most popular crops. Others include barley, corn, millet, lentils, tomatoes, potatoes, melons, onions, olives, grapes, apples, citrus. Tobacco is grown in Latakia. Cotten and lentils are the largest exports. [39]
  • Forestry: About 2.7% of Syria is forested. Thus, most of their lumber is imported. [39,40]
  • Fishing: Syria has small fisherman population. Sardines, tuna, groupers, tunny, red mullet, and gray mullet. [39]
  • Livestock: Sheep, cattle, camels, and poultry[39]
Resources
  • Mining: Major limestone quarries near Damascus and Aleppo. Basalt is used for roads and quarried near Homs and Aleppo. Other mining includes marl, phosphate ore, iron ore, rock salt, asphalt, gypsum, chrome, and magnanese. [41]
  • Petroleum: Major oil fields extended from Iraq near Dayr al-Zawr are Sawaydiyyah, Qaratshuk, and Rumaylan. From 1974-1990 petro was Syria's leading natural resource and export. [41]
  • Natural Gas: Since 1940, production has grown to become a major energy export.[41]
  • Power: Due to increased industrialization and lacking infrustructure, Syria struggles with domestic electricity shortages. Most electricity is generated by thermal stations powered by oil and natural gas. Some hydroelectric power on the Euphrates Dam too. [41]
Industry
  • Textiles: Wool, cotton, nylon, natural silk. [42]
  • Manufacturing: Chemical and industrial engineering products such as cement, glass panes, bottles, utensils, pharmaceuticals, plywood, and batteries. Also preparation of superphosphates, urea, and petroleum refining.[42]
  • Food stuffs: Salt, vegetable oils, cotton cake, canned fruit/veggies, tobacco, and dairy products. [42]
Finance
  • National currency: Syrian pound, 1 USD is 13001.13 SYP.[43,44]
  • Central Bank of Syria: Controls all other banks (banking is nationalized) and issues Syrian pound. [43,45]
  • Commercial Bank of Syria: Finances trade, markets products, and does foreign-exchange.[43]
  • Real Estate Bank: Finances construction and does all normal banking operations. [43]
  • Popular Credit Bank: Makes loans to manufacturers, craftsmen, and co-ops. [43]
  • Other banks: Has a development bank, agricultral bank, and nationalized insurance company. Also have a number of private banks since banking was reformed in 2000. [43]
  • Stock exchange: Damascus Securities Exchange. [43]
  • Foreign aid: During cold war they were aided by China, Bulgaria, and the USSR. Today they still receive lost of aid from China. After the first Gulf War, they got $2.4 billion in aid from Saudi Arabia for their support. France, the EU, the US and other states have provided over $50 billion to Syria since the 2011 civil war broke out. [43,46,48,49]
  • [43]
Trade
  • Balance of Trade: Import more than they export. This used to be offset by tourist, foreign aid, and overseas workers but after the Syrian civil war GDP plummeted. Trade is controlled by the state. [49]
  • Imports: Indistrial and agricultral machinery, vehicles, pharmaceuticals, food, and fabric. China (11%), Egypt (5%), Italy (4%), UAE (4%), Lebanon (3%), Russia (3%), Ukraine (3%).[49]
  • Exports: Petroleum, phosphates, ginned cotton, cotton seeds, barley, lentils, cottone/woolen fabrics, dried fruit, veggies, skins, and raw wool. Saudi Arabia (17%), Lebanon (12%), Egypt (12%), UAE (11%), Iraq (8%), Croatia (3%), Jordan (3%). [49]
Services
  • Serivces: Makes up 50% of the workforce. [50]
  • Tourism: Tourist attractions include historical ancient ruins and historical sites, including some UNESCO World Heritage sites. Tourists come from Arab states, Iran, and Turkey during the summer. In 1990s tourism sector was privatized, which stimulated growth.[50]
Labor
  • General Federation of Workers: Extremely powerful labor union for industrial employees and is responsible for many social services. [51]
  • General Federation of Trade Unions: All unions are controlled by the state controlled GFTU. Other trade orgs include the General Federation of Farmers, federation for artisans, federation for vocational workers, and associations for professionals [51,52]
  • Worker Laws: Minimum wage is 185,940 SYP (14 USD) per month. Laws for child labor and worker-employee relations. However, laws are not always enforced due to poor economic conditions. [51,54]
  • Wages: Heavy industry make the most and textiles make the least. Most workers spend all their money on housing and food. [51]
  • Taxation: Income taxes make up 33% of government revenue. Other revenue is derived from indirect and direct taxes. [51]
Transport/
Telecoms
  • Roads: Major form of transportation. Main road from Aleppo to Damascus and Damascus to Baghdad. [54]
  • Railways: Northern railway from Aleppo to Turkey back into Al-Hasakah then to Baghdad. The Hejaz Railway runs from Damascus to Amman (Jordan) and was the main railroad of the Ottomans. They also have major railroads from Aleppo to Lebanon and Aleppo to Damascus. Smaller ones are between Homs and Lebanon, Beirut and Damascus, Latakia to Aleppo/Al-Hasakah, and Al-Qamishli (in Al-Hasakah), and others that lead out of the country. [54,55]
  • Ports: Latakia and Tartus are the main ports. The UN oil for food program boosted Syrian shipping during the 1990s. Latakia is the main port and exports bitumen/asphalt, cereals, cotton, fruit, eggs, vegetable oil, pottery, and tobacco. Tartus is the second largest port. [54,56,57]
  • Airports: International airports in Aleppo and Damascus. Domestic airports in Al-Qamishli, Latakia, Dayr al-Zawr, and Tadmur. The Syrian Arab Airlines provides internationald and domestic travel. [54]

Government

Constitution
  • Constitutional Framework: Established in 1973. Is a unitary multiparty republic with one legislative house. Says Syria is an integral part of the Arab homeland, the people hold all legislative power, and guarantees freedom of expression/equality. [58]
  • Enforcement: Under Hafez al-Assad the constitution was not enforced well and rights were increasingly suppressed. [58]
  • President: Is the head of state and executive body (called the Council of Ministers). Must be Muslim. Serves for 7 year terms, no term limits. Appoints the Council of Ministers (Syrian cabinet), the prime minister, and military officers. Is nominated by the Baath Party and approved by Parliament. [89]
  • Council of Ministers: Executive body/cabinet. Consists for prime minister, deputy prime minister, and the ministers. [89]
  • Arab Socialist Baath Party: Elects the head of state (must be Muslim) and the cabinet. The cabinet has legislative and executive powers. [58]
  • People's Assembly of Syria: Syria's unicameral legislature. Elected by popular vote to a 4-year term. The Baath Party is guaranteed an absolute majority by the constitution. 250 members. Two main fronts are the National Progressive Front (includes the Baath party) and the Popular Front for Change and Liberation. [58,59]
Local Government
  • Governorates: Syria is divided into governorates. Governorates are divides into districts (manatiq). Districts are divided into subdistricts (nawahi). [60]
  • Leadership: Governors (muhafizun) have some power in their governorates but local government is subject to the control of the minister of the interior of the federal government.[60]
Justice
  • Judiciary: Summary courts hear civil, commerial, and penal cases. Criminal cases are heard by First Instance Court, each governorate has a First Instance Court. Appeals are heard in an appeal court, each capital of each governorate has an appeals court. Damascus has a high appeals court, constitutional court, military tribunal court, and mufti's court for Islamic law. There are also multiple non-Muslim courts for personal-status cases. [61]
  • Law code: Syrian law is derived first from Islamic law and second from French civil law. [61]
Political Process
  • Elections: Universal adult sufferage but not seen as free/fair. 13% of parliment is women and the Alawites also dominate Syrian politics. [62]
  • Arab Socialist Baath Party: Since the 1940s, the Baath Party has been the ruling party due to multiple coups. The Baaths have branches in many other Arab states too. The Central Command is the leading organ of the party and was formed by the merging of the National Command and the Regional Command. The Central Command nominates the president and is the most powerful institution in Syria. The Contral Command is lead by the supreme national leadership of each regional branch, who are elected by their congresses. The regional leader of Syria is the second most powerful position, behind the national leadership (presidency). [62,63,64]
  • National Progressive Front: All political parties are linked together under the NPF. The Baaths dominate the NPF. [62]
Security
  • Military: It is compulsory for all adult males to be in the military, college students do not have to though. Military service provides general/technical education.[65]
  • Army: Largest force of the military. Defense, public works, road construction, and public health.[65]
  • Air Force and Navy: Small air force and navy. [65]
  • Palestinian Guerrillas: There are also multiple Palestinian guerrilla forces in Syria including the Al-Saiqah organization, the PFLP, and Palestinian fedayeen. [65,66,67,68]
  • Law enforcment: Government has judicial and executive institutions but they are prone to corruption and are highly politicized. Large human trafficing market, usually from Iraq and the Phillipines. Major source for forced labor, sexual exploitation, and domestic servitude. Trafficked children are used as human-shields and suicide bombers. Illicit-arms market is also one of the world's largest, concentrated in Kurdish areas and south Syria. Syria has a lucrative oil smuggling market from Iran and Lebanon due to high demand and prices. Hezbollah traffics heroina and cocaine through Syria and is the main foreign organized criminal actor within Syria. Illicit cannabis trade is also widespread and is usually grown within the counrty. Widespread illicit finance sector for tax avoidance purposes. Murder rate is 3, 60 prisoners per 100k people, 7.07/10 on the global crime index (11th internationally). [86,87,88]
Health and Welfare
  • Health: State and private hospitals, hospitals/outpatient clinics for the armed forces, also public/private outpatient clinics. Child mortality mainly from measles and digestive/respiratory diseases. Tuberculosis and trachoma are common, especially among Bedouin and the poor. Health conditions in cities are in good conditions. Most homes/buildings have running water. Most doctors are concentrated in large cities. [69]
  • Welfare: Mininstry of Social Welfare and Labour is responsible for finding work and providing welfare for the unemployed. They also sponsor youth activities. The government provides large cash infusions to private welfare groups. Syria has a high birthrate and has caused the standard of living to decrease for rural dwellers. [69]
Housing
  • Structures: Old homes are built of stone, new buildings are built of concrets, mosques and official buildings are build of hewn stone. [70]
  • Housing shortage: Mass migration, especially of young males, from rural areas to cities has led to a housing crisis. [70]
  • Ministry of Municipal and Rural Affairs: Government housing for low-income in cities. [70]
Education
  • Lower Education: Literacy rate is 92% for males and 81% for females. Mandatory education for primary (6 year), optional for lower secondary (3 years) and higher secondary (3 years). Secondary school can either be university prep or vocational with emphasis on technical education. 98% of the schools are state ran. The Baath party heavily influences the primary schooling system. [71,72,73]
  • Universities: 43% of the population goes to post-secondary school. University of Damascus, University of Aleppo, Tishrin University (Latakia), Al-Baath University (Homs), are all public universities. Damascus University is Syria's best university and ranks 3687 worldwide. [71,74]

Culture

Culture
  • Cultural milieu: Arab, Mediterranean, and European elements. Syrians closely follow international poltitcs and culture. [75]
  • The Ministry of Culture and National Guidance: In charge of promoting and directing Syrian culture. Main objective is to promote an Arab national character. [75,76]
Society
  • Family Structure: The family is the core of social life. Parents have large say over marriage decisions. Marriage is usually within the same religion but Muslim men may marry non-Muslim women, the reverse is illegal though. People from different religions are usually friends with each other too. [77]
  • Clothing: Eclectic clothing culture. Some people dress like westerners/Europeans and others dress in veils and traditional clothing. [77]
  • Holidays: Muslims celebrate Ramadan, Eid al-Fitr, and Eid al-Adha. Christians celebrate Christmas and Easter. [77]
  • Cuisine: Garlic, onions, and spices are staples. Kibbeh, ground beef/lamb/spices/pine nuts inside a diamond cut shell. Grapevine leaves stuffed with spiced lamb/beef and rice cooked in lemon juice. Meat pies and spinach pies. Yogurt, fruits, vegatables, flat bread, cheeses, salads, olives. Syrian pastries are also numerous. [77]
Arts
  • Painting and sculpture: Until after WWI, public art was restricted to geometric and animal designs. Sculptures are in white marbles usually. Mosques, palaces, and public buildings, especially in Damascus are filled with sculptures and art. [78]
  • Writing: Nizar Qabbani was a Syrian diplomat and poet, originally only romantic work but later turned to politics and women's rights in the Muslim world. Adonis was born in Syria. He was a poet, literary critic, and leader of the modernist movement in Arabic poetry. [78,79,80]
  • Theatre: Leading playwrite is Sadallah Wannus, made political plays. The National Theatre gives regular performances. Comic/wise-fool figure, Ghawwar, made by Durayad Lahham, was a large following in the Arab world. [78]
  • Music: Renowned Syrian singer and ud player Farid al-Atrash and his sister Ashmahan (Amal) who was a singer and actor are very popular. [78]
Institutions
  • Museums: The government promotes national folk traditions and have a national folk museum in Damascus. Damascus also has the National Museum for Syrian history. Aleppo has an archaeological museum. [81]
  • Libraries: Al-Assad National Library, al-Zahiriyyah library, and the University of Damascus library. [81]
  • Academies Arab institute of music teaches arts. Arabic Language Academy in Damascus. [81]
  • UNESCO World Heritage sites: Ancient cities of Damascus, Aleppo, and Bostra, the site of Palmyra, and the fortresses Krak des Chevaliers and Qalat Salah al-Din. Krak des Chevaliers means Castle of the Knights and is the greatest fortress built by the European crusaders in Syria that showcases medieval military architecture. [81,82]
Sports
  • Sports: Soccer is most popular and they watch Arab and EU teams. In the cities gyms are popular with weight lifting, judo, and karate. Stadiums are in Damascus, Aleppo, and Latakia. The Institue for Sports Education is state ran and runs sporting events. The General Union of Sport is state-funded and runs sports in rural areas. First Olympic medal in men's heavyweight freestyle wrestling in 1984. [83]
  • Recreation: Families enjoy picnics by streams and trips to mountain resorts. [83]
Media
  • Publishing: Mostly in Damascus. All newspapers are subject to government restrictions. Leading daily newspapers are Tishrin, Al-Bath, and the government ran Al-Thawrah. The Syrian Arab News Agency (SANA) is the official state-ran news agency and gives the media government propaganda to spread. Syria ranks 175/180 on the World Press Freedom Index due to reporters having restricted access to territories from the civil war, the Assad regime uses the media to disseminate Baathist ideology and does not allow any pluralism. Many journalists have been exiled as a result of a Syrian amendment saying "spreading false news online that damages the prestige of the nation" can be prosecuted. [84,85]
  • Radio: Overseen by the Directorate-General of Radio and Television. Mainly in Arabic but also in English, French, Turkish, Russian, Hebrew, and German. First private radio station was Al-Madina FM. [84]
  • Television: Overseen by the Directorate-General of Radio and Television. Syrian Television Service reaches the largest amount of people. Broadcasts include educational, cultural, drama, music, news, and sports. Syrian TV series are also beginning to gain popularity in the Arab world. Government used to have full control but satellite dishes have opened up Syrians to more Middle Eastern and European broadcasting. [84]

1970-2000

Hafez al-Assad Regime

  • The authoritarian regime under Assad was a cult of personality around him and gained popularity due to their polices of economic development, land reform, promotion of education, strengthening of the military, and vehement opposition to Israel.
  • As president, Hafez brought stability to Syria and established it as a major power in the Middle East
  • Assad reverted the radical socialist policies of the Jadid regime and strengthened privatization in the economy after taking over in 1971, despite the 1973 consitution only allowing for public, collective and individual property
  • Assads foreign policy consisted of strengthening relations with the Soviets to develop the Syrian economy/military, normalizing relations with Egypt/Saudi Arabia to establish a "Cairo-Damascus-Riyadh axis" against Israel
  • Due to this and harsh repression of anti-Baathist opponents, the Assad government saw large stability in Syria with the alliance of the Baath Party, the military, and the bureaucracy (which was led by Assad and supported by and Alawite network of officials and officers)
  • The majority Sunni population outside of Damascus and in merchant groups were the main opponents of the Baathist-Alawite government
  • Hafez's foreign policy was marked by wars with Israel during the 1960s-70s, a rivalry with Baathists Iraq, and intervention in Lebanon.
  • Arab nationalism flourished under Assad, emphasizing the Palestinain problem, socialism, and anti-imperialism and was brought to the cities and sponsored in media (movies, TV, radio) by the Baath government
  • In the 1980s, Syria's economy began to stagnate and saw inflation and government corruption due to a weak private sector and military spending, especially in Lebanon
  • Assad turned to the oil rich states in the Middle East for aid and turned to China for military supplies after the USSR fell in 1991.

Black September

Corrective Movement

  • After Syria failed in their intervention in the Black September crisis in Jordan, Assad began planning to overthrow Jadid

  • Assad also purged all As-Saiqa leaders from the Baathist government
  • On November 13, Assad proclaimed leadership over Syria
  • After taking power, Assad ended the Jadid era doctrine of exporting revolutions and shifted to an emphasis on defeating Israel with the help of the Soviet Union and shifting toward a "nationalist socialist" ideology

1973

1973 Syrian Constitution

  • The 1973 constitution reinforced the the leading role of the Baathist Party in Syria and transformed the revolutionary state into a Presidential Monarchy in which all power was consalidated under the Syrian President (Assad) in a one-party (Baathist) socialist state
  • Under the new consitution, the President served for 7-year terms with no term limits and was nominated by the Baath Party and approved by parliament
  • The consitution not requiring the President to be Muslim resulted in riots in Hama, Homs, and Aleppo organized by the Muslim Brotherhood and the ulama
  • In March 1973, Assad folded to the pressure and returned the Muslim President requirement then obtained a fatwa from a Lebanese cleric proclaiming that Alawites were a part of Shia Islam
  • The consitution also emphasized pan-Arab ideology and positioned Syria as one region in the wider Arab nation and calls out the need to protect against imperialism and Zionism
  • This gave Assad full control over the military, party, and bureaucracy
  • Assad further strengthened his power by assigning Alawite loyalists to high ranking positions in various state institutions

October 1973

Yom Kippur War

  • In 1967 during the six day war, Israel captured half of the Golan Heights from Syria
  • Following the six day war, the Arab states would hold the position that were would be no peace, no recognition, and no negotiations with Israel
  • The Yom Kippur War was from October 6-25, 1973, led by a coalition of Arab states (Egypt, Syria, Jordan) headed by Syria and Egypt against Israel
  • In the weeks before the war, Syrian troops were detected on the border and Israel sent reinforcements to the Golan Heights
  • Initially, Israel was going to attack Syria preemtively but decided against it (in fear of losing US support) and then the war officially began when Egypt and Syria suprise attacked Israel (on Yom Kippur, CIA even failed to predict the attack) by invading the Sinai and the Golan Heights (which were captured in the 1967 war)
  • During the war, Egypt unexpectedly ceased hostilities exposing the Syrian front, this made Assad resent Anwar Sadat until his death.
  • The IDF initially sustained heavy loses against the Syrian forces and lost the southern Golan Heights on October 8 (Israel even considered using nukes in fear of total defeat, until the US resupplied them on Oct 14), but by Oct 10 Israel had recaptured the Golan territory and pushed the Syrians back across the Purple Line (pre-war ceasefire line)
  • On Oct. 11, Israel chose to push deeper into Syria and reached within 30km of Damascus but were eventually halted by Iraqi and Jordanian counterattacks from within Syria on October 12-21
  • The Egyptians and Syrians were both being funnelled arms by the Soviets (starting Oct 9 and some Soviet personnel even fought alongside the Syrians) and Israel by the US (starting Oct 14)
  • An initial UN ceasefire was brokered on October 22 (resolution 338) but fighting continued with Israel pushing to the outskirts of Cairo
  • This forced a second ceasefire on October 25 that ended the war due to the dangerously heightened tensions between the US and the Soviets
  • In December 1973 there was a peace summit in Geneva between Israel and the Arab states mediated by the Soviets and the US but the confrence ended when Syria refused attendance
  • Syrian and Israeli forces continued to fight in an attrition war into 1974 until a final disengagement agreement was agreed upon due to Kissinger's shuttle diplomacy in May 1974 (official end of the Yom Kippur War)
  • Under the agreement, Israel gave back mount Hermon and the Quneitra to Syria and pulled back to the Purple Line, however Israel now controlled more territory than after 1967.
  • A UN buffer zone was established by the UN Disengagement and Observer Force (UNDOF) in the Golan Heights and the agreement has remained in place until today
  • Syrian Casualties: 3000-3500 dead
  • Syrian war crimes: summarily executed (many blindfolded with tied hands) and tortured Israeli prisoners (electric shock to the genitials, fingernails ripped off, human ashtrays)

Islamic Uprising

  • This culminated in the Hama uprising in 1964 led by Brotherhood
  • In the aftermath of the Hama revolt being crushed the Fighting Vanguard was formed
  • The Fighting Vanguard was led by Marwan Hadid and was a spliter group of the Brotherhood due to the Brotherhood's rejection of Hadid's calls for jihad against the Baathists
  • In March 1973, Assad's new consitution added further to the Muslim anger after it didn't require the President to be Muslim
  • After the October war in 1973, Syria also fell into an economic downturn
  • In June 1976, Hadid was captured and died in prison and the remaining members of the Vanguard vowed to avenge his dead
  • Later in 1976, the Vanguard would carry out terror attacks, including explosions and assassinations aimed at prominent military/government officers/bureaucrats, most of which were Alawite
  • This resulted in a series of indiscriminate jihadist attacks on the minority Alawite community
  • Throughout the uprising, the insurgents would be supported with arms and supplies by the Iraqi government of Saddam Hussein
  • Jisr ash-Shughur massacre: in March 1980 a bunch of Jisr ash-Shughur residents marched on the Baath headquarters and set it on fire, the military responded by arresting 200 people then executing almost all of them
  • The insugency concluded in 1982 following the Hama massacre when the Brotherhood was defeated

Aleppo Artillery School Massacre

  • Starting in November 1979, the Brotherhood terror attacks on Baath party officials/targets became almost a daily occurence (mostly in Aleppo and other northern cities)

  • Between 1979-1981 the Aleppo crackdowns resulted in ~2000 deaths (many civilians indiscriminatly) and 8000 arrests; Brotherhood attacks resulted in 300 Baath/Alawite deaths

Hama Massacres

  • In April 1981, Islamic guerillas staged a failed attack on an Alawite village near Hama

  • The 1982 Hama massacre marked the end of the Islamic uprising as the Brotherhood would operate in exile following it (mainly in Iran)

June 1982

1982 Lebanon War

  • In 1976, Israel began supporting Lebanese Christians in South Lebanon against the PLO
  • Israel supported the Christian Maronite Phalange party led by Bashir Gemayel, who's strategy was to provoke Israel by provoking Syria into retalitory attacks on Christians
  • In June 1982, Israel invaded southern Lebanon, attacking the PLO following an assassination attempt on the Israeli ambassador to the UK by an Iraq-backed group Abu Nidal (who were actually enemies with the PLO)
  • At the time, Begin was the PM of Israel and publicly stated they didn't want any confrontation with Syria, only to drive out the PLO
  • However, the driving force behind the Israeli invasion was Ariel Sharon and one of his many reasons for invading included driving out the Syrian Army from Lebanon, who backed the PLO and who had over 30,000 troops stationed in Lebanon at the time.
  • Thus, the Syrian and IDF clash was inevitable and planned from the start by Sharon
  • One IDF division was sent to the Bekaa Valley, to (and succeeded) prevent Syrian reinforcements and prevent Syrian forces from being able to intervene in the Israeli invasion.
  • On June 8, 1982, the IDF and Syrian army battled in the Battle of Jezzine in central Lebanon, with the IDF winning that same day.
  • After the battle, Assad was convinced that the IDF wanted to fight the Syrians too, not just the PLO as they had publicly stated and sped up the deployment of Syrian forces in the Bekaa Valley
  • Israel saw that the Syrians were only brining forces from the Golan Heights as a sign that Syria didn't want a wider was and decided to launch an operation on Syria the next day
  • On June 9, 1982, Israel launched Operation Mole Cricket 19 on the Syria forces, which was one of the largest air battles since WW2 and was the first time in history that a western-equipped air force destroyed a Soviet SAM (surface to air missile) network, resulted in a dramatic victory for Israel.
  • On June 10, Reagan imposed on Begin and Assad to agree to a ceasefire but it fell apart.
  • Israeli advances deeper into Lebanon would continue to defeat the PLO and Syrian forces until June 11 there was an Israeli-Syrian ceasefire that was extended to the PLO on June 12 but it fell apart on June 13.
  • On June 15, Israel cut the Beirut-Damascus highway, cutting off any Syrian reinforcements and offered Syrian forces in Beirut to withdraw to the Bekaa Valley but the Syrian government refused and fighting along the highway continued from June 16-22.
  • On June 22, Israel launched an operation to fully capture the highway and by June 25, they had forced the Syrians to withdraw and a ceasefire was announced.
  • During the war, the IAF saw great success and dominated the Syrians, while tank battles were more evenly matched
  • In September 1982, Bachir Gemayel, the newly elected president of Lebanon and former Christian Phalangists commander, was assassinated in a bombing by a member of the Syrian Social Nationalist Party (who supported "Greater Syria")
  • In May 1983 Lebanon and Israel agreed on an Israeli withdraw from Lebanon under the condition that Syrian troops leave but under Syrian pressure Lebanon backed out in March 1984
  • In January 1985, Israel began a phased withdraw from Lebanon back to a security zone in south Lebanon, they would occupy south Lebanon for the next 20 years
  • After the Israelis withdrew to the security zone was finished in June 1985, the Syrians gained de facto occupation of North Lebanon and Assad began to slowly re-grow his limited control over Lebanon

1976-2005

Syrian Occupation of Lebanon

  • The occupation began in May 1976, when Syria intervened in the Lebanese Civil War; Syria sent 2,000 troops into Lebanon and the occupation was eventually legitimized by the Arab League, who allowed the Arab Deterrent Force in Lebanon as a peacekeeping force (primarily composed of Syrian troops)
  • During the occupation, Assad spread the idea of "Greater Syria" and took steps to integrate Lebanon into Syria
  • Hafez quote July 1976: "Syria and Lebanon were one state and one people... and have shared interests and a common history"
  • Hafez quote 1983: "Lebanon and Syria are one single people, one single nation. We may be divided into two independent states, but that does not mean that we are two different nations... I would even argue that the feeling of kinship between Syria and Lebanon runs deeper than it does between states in the United States... Soviet Union is not in Lebanon and never was present there, neither before nor after the Israeli invasion."
  • In 1989, the Lebanese Civil War ended and Beirut was split between the rival parties
  • East Beirut was controlled by Michel Aoun (anti-Syrian and military government) and West Beirut was controlled by Selim el-Hoss (supported by Syria and civilian government)
  • In March 1989, the "War of Liberation" was started by Aoun to rid Lebanon of the Syrians but was defeated and exiled in October 1990 after losing international support (much of Aoun's support was from Iraq, who sent aid from 1988 until Iraq was invaded in 1990)
  • In October 1989, the Lebanese Civil war ended with the signing of the the Syrian-sponsored Taif Agreement, which provided for a large role for Syria in Lebanese affairs including foreign policy and economic matters.
  • After the war ended, about 1 million Syrians went to Lebanon to find jobs and in 1994, the Lebanese government controversially granted citizenship to over 200,000 Syrians
  • In 1991, this paved the way for Lebanon and Syria to sign the treaty of "Brotherhood, Cooperation, and Coordination," legitimizing Syrian military presence and giving them responsibility to defend Lebanon
  • In October 1991, Syria launched a major operation both army and airforce, into Lebanon against Aoun in his stronghold of the presidential palace, killing hundreds of Aoun supporters and forcing Aoun into exile in Paris
  • The Syrian operation was only able to take place after the US convinced Israel not to interfere with Syrian forces in Beirut, in exchange for Syria helping in the Gulf War against Saddam Hussein.
  • The Syrian occupation of Lebanon was a proxy war between all the militias in Lebanon who were being backed by Iran (Hezbollah), the US (Syria, Israel), and the Soviets (National Movement).
  • Hafez was the only Arab leader to occupy another Arab country for 30 years and subjegated not only oppressed the Syrians but also the Lebanese, by trying to force a common thought and culture onto them through heavy governmental influence and pressure
  • The Syrian occupation was particularly disadventageous to the Maronite population, since most of their leadership was either killed, jailed, or exiled.

April 2005

Syrian Withdraw from Lebanon

  • In 2000, Israel withdrew from southern Lebanon and Hafez died, resulting in a strong resistance to Syrian presence from the Lebanese population
  • In August 2001, the first anti-Syrian demonstrations in Lebanon began and turned violent, resulting in the Lebanese-Syrian security system brutally repressing the revolt, with arrests of hundreds of people (mainly Christians) without justification.
  • In September 2004, UNSC resolution 1559 was adopted (coauthored by US/France) calling for free elections in Lebanon, the disarming of militias like Hezbollah, and all foreign forces (Syria) to withdraw and cease intervening in Lebanese politics.
  • In February 2005, Lebanese PM Rafic Hariri (anti-Syrian), was assassinated in a truck bomb attack.
  • The Lebanese government accused 6 Austrialian nationals of carrying out the attack but they were later absolved.
  • Despite non-substantial evidence for any party having carried out the attacks, the Lebanese population largely blamed Syria government and the pro-Syrian Lebanese president Emile Lahoud for the assassination.
  • This sparked Cedar Revolution with mass nonvoilent/peaceful protests with the goals of: (1) full Syrian withdraw from Lebanon, (2) independent leadership and parliement elections free from Syrian influence, (3) an independent commission to investigate Hariri's death
  • Most of the international community called for Syria to withdraw and implement UN resolution 1559
  • Hezbollah was against resolution 1559 and led mass pro-Syrian protests with hundreds of thousands of (mostly Shite) protesters.
  • Daily protests says large getherings of over 25,000 people and, unlike the 1990s anti-Syrian demonstrations, were not met with force from the government.
  • On February 28 2005, the pro-Syrian government under PM Omar Karami resigned and called for new elections.
  • In April 2005, all Syrian occupation forces had completed the full withdrawl from Lebanon and implemented resolution 1559, due to the Cedar Revolution
  • Also in April 2005, a general election was held, and the Special Tribunal for Lebanon was created to investigate Hariri's death; achieving all the main goals of the revolution
  • In October 2008, Syria and Lebanon established dimplomatic relations and opened embassies for the first time in history.

1998-2012

Relations with Turkey

  • Syria and Turkey had traditionally had sour relations due to the self-annexation of the Hatay Province (Alexandretta) in 1939, water diputes from dams built on the Euphrates and Tigris rivers during the Southeastern Anatolia Project, and Syrian support for the Kurdistan Worker's Party (PKK)

  • In 1978, Ocalan founded the PKK in Turkey and in 1979, he departed from Turkey for Syria after establishing connections with militant Pal orgs
  • In the early 1980s, the PKK spread further into Iraq and in 1984 launched an armed resistance against Turkey from Iraq that would turn into an all-out war
  • In October 1998, relations with Turkey improved somewhat when the leader of the PKK, Abdullah Ocalan, was expelled by Syrian authorities
  • In Janurary 2004, Bashar was the first Syrian president to visit Ankara and later in 2004 Erdogan came to Damascus to sign a joint free trade agreement
  • In April 2007, Assad and Erdogan met again at the ceremony of hte Aleppo International Stadium and in 2005 Turkish President Sezer visited Damascus during the Cedar Revolution
  • In 2008, Turkey was invited to mediate talks between Irael and Syria over the Golan Heights, the talks ended up failing
  • In April 2009, Turkey and Syrian had an unprecedented three-day military exercise along their shared border

March 2011-June 2012

Syrian Revolution

  • Leading up to the Arab Spring, Syria was considered the most restrictive police state in the Arab World
  • This included more than 400 travel bans on individuals that were political opponents to Assad, aresting of journalists, and tightening of internet censorship
  • Social discontent was also caused by the US invasion of Iraq, the most intense drought ever recorded in the region, and widespread corruption (nepotism and bribery) within the Baathist government
  • The protesters demanded the end of the state of emergency, more freedoms, and an end to pervasive government corruption
  • In March 2011, as part of the larger Arab Spring, popular discontent within the Baathist government led to large scale pro-democracy/anti-Assad rallies and protests accross Syria
  • The protests originated on March 6 2011 in Daraa following the detainment of 15 young students who were spray painting anti-government graffiti
  • The Daraa protests quickly spread and they eventually burned down the Baath party headquarters and other buildings, with security forces responding by firing live ammunition at crowds
  • By late March 2011, the protests had spread to Damascus, al-Hasakah, Deir az-Zor, Hama, Homs, Aleppo, and Latakia
  • However, the Syrian Revolution protests were violently suppressed by the Assad regime and tens of thousands of deaths and detentions occured following an official "shoot to kill" policy by the government, deploying snipers/heavy machine guns/shelling
  • Assad also imposed a complete media blackout, banning independent news coverage and banning any foreign press
  • Mass defections occured after security forces who disagreed with the government policy against the demonstrations were themselves fired upon by the Baathist paramilitaries and Shabiha death squads (Alawite loyalists executioners)
  • In April 2011, the Baath regime announced concessions such as political reforms and policy changes as well has a lift of the state of emergency (used for arbitrary arrests and political supression), but the protests continued
  • After the failed concessions, in late April 2011 Assad deployed the Syrian Army and Shabiha mercenaries on the protesters using tanks and live ammunition to unleash violence on the civilians, who continued to demonstrate in larger and larger protests
  • In July 2011, the early insurgency phase of the Syrian civil war began with the formation of the FSA
  • From August 2011 to June 2012, anti-government demonstrations continued and were met with repression
  • In June 2012, the revolution had transformed into a full-blown civil war
  • Between 2011 and 2012, more than 60,000 Syrian civilians had been killed by the Syrian military forces

July 2011-April 2012

Syrian Civil War Insurgency Phase

  • After the failed concessions and repressions by Assad in April 2011, the anti-government demonstrations continued and were met with further repression
  • In July 2011, the Free Syrian Army (FSA) was formed by 7 defected Syrian Army officers and composed of defected soldiers, marking the beginning of armed insurgency against the Syrian Arab Republic
  • The FSA's stated goal was to bring down the Assad government
  • In August 2011, Syrian opposition factions and dissidents formed an anti-Assad coalition called the Syrian National Council and political branch of the FSA based in Turkey
  • In October 2011, the FSA began recieving active support from the Turkish government
  • Later in October 2011, there were massive pro-Assad rallies with over an estimated million demostrators
  • In September-October 2011, the Syrian Army launched an offensive with tanks and helicopters on Homs
  • In November 2011, the Syrian Army and FSA clashes in Homs as the government siege continued
  • In Janurary 2012, Assad began using large-scale artillery operations against the FSA leading to the destruction of civilian homes from indiscriminate shelling
  • In Febryary 2012, the Syrian Army launched a major offensive in Homs to retake rebel-held territories
  • By March 2012, the Syrian Army forces had taken back 70% of Homs and also the city of Idlib
  • In April 2012, Assad began deploying attack helicopters against the rebels
  • Later in April 2012, a UN backed ceasefire was declared but quickly failed.

April 2012-December 2013

Escalation of Syrian Civil War

  • Due to the Syrian FSA insurgency raging on for over a year, the Arab League and the UN moved to form a peace plan
  • In February 2012, the Arab League and the UN appointed Kofi Annan as their special envoy to Syria
  • In March 2012, Kofi submitted a 6 point peace plan to the UNSC and in late March, it was announced that Assad had accpeted the peace plan
  • In early April, "both parties appeared to be observing the ceasefire" but shelling started again in late April 2012 and by May 2012, both the FSA and the Syrian government were violating the ceasefire
  • On 25 May 2012, the Houla massacre took place, where 108 people were summarily executed, resulting in the FSA ultimatum to the Baath government and calling the ceasefire "dead" as they began mounting a nationwide offensive on the government troops
  • By June 2012, the Kofi peace initiative had fully fallen apart, with both sides openly engaging in violence again
  • Also in June 2012, Assad vowed to crush the anti-government uprisings and shortly after fighting broke out in Latakia
  • On 12 June 2012, the UN for the first time officially proclaimed Syria to be in a state of civil war
  • Later in June 2012, peaceful protests spread to Aleppo and Damascus and then a Turkish fighter jet was shot down by the Syrian government killing both pilots and forcing Assad to apologise.
  • In July 2012, the Syrian Government took parts of Homs, Idlib and Damascus and fighting intensified in Aleppo, where Syrian government forces used fighter jets on the rebels
  • In September 2012, the Syrian Army killed 21 Kurds in Aleppo, causing the Kurdish YPG to retaliate against the Syrian Army
  • Also in September 2012, the FSA siezed a border crossing in Raqqa between Syria and Turkey, who was supporting the rebels
  • In October 2012, the rebel forces took parts of Idlib and the highway linking Damascus to Aleppo
  • In late October 2012, Lakhdar Brahimi (Kofi replacement) arranged a ceasefire that quickly fell apart
  • After the ceasefire fell, the Syrian Army moved on Damascus and the rebels made gains in Idlib, Aleppo, Deir ez-Zor
  • In December 2012, the Syrian Army began using Scud missiles on the rebels but the rebels still made gains in Damascus and Idlib and launched an offensive in Homs
  • In Jan-Feb 2013, the rebels made gains in Idlib, Raqqa, Aleppo, and Al-Hasakah, the Syrian Army also began shelling Hezbollah targets, and the Syrian Army engaged in fighting with the Kurdish YPG
  • In March 2013, the rebels captured Raqqa city, which was first provincial capital to fall and the Syrian Army first started shelling rebel targets in Lebanon
  • From April-June 2013, the Syrian Government forces, with the help of Hezbollah, launched an offensive and retook territory around Damascus, Idlib, Homs, Daara
  • From July-October 2013, fighting continued with increased activity from ISIS and other jihadist groups (FSA formally declared war on ISIS in June 2013) as well as the YPG and other Kurdish groups and with the FSA and Syrian Army trading territories in a stalemate
  • In August 2013, the Syrian Army used a chemical attack near Damascus leading to hundreds of deaths
  • From Oct-Dec 2013, the Syrian Army with allies Hezbollah and the al-Abas brigade, lauched an offensive on Damascus and Aleppo and recaptured territories in both regions
  • In November 2013, the Islamic rebels took the al-Omar oil field (largest in Syria) forcing the government to almost entirely rely on imported oil

Jan 2014

Inter-rebel Conflict in Syrian Civil War

  • Starting in around January 2014, the FSA and other Syrian opposition factions began fighting amongst each other and among various Islamic groups
  • In 2012 and 2013, the FSA had been in battles with both al-Qaeda and the Al-Nusra Front (Front for the Conquest of Levant or Jabhat al Nusra)
  • The Supreme Military Council was the highest military officers in the FSA and included the Islamic Front (Ahrar ash-Sham, al-Tawhid Brigade and Jaysh al-Islam) until they withdrew in Dec 2013 due to fighting
  • Between 2014-2016, fighting between FSA groups and Islamic front groups continued battling in Northern Aleppo
  • In October 2014, the AL-Nusra Front and Jund al-Aqsa (Liwa al-Aqsa) clashed with the Syrian Revolutionaries Front (SRF), part of the FSA, in Idlib
  • Also in Oct 2014, Al-Nusra also attacked the Hazm Movement (FSA affiliated group) in Aleppo and the fighting between the two spread to Idlib by Jan 2015
  • In Nov 2014, Ahrar al-Sham raided Jaysh al-Islam positions.
  • In June 2015, the Southern Front (part of FSA) rejected cooperation with al-Nusra depite having previously cooperated with al-Queda
  • In October 2015, the Southern Front and the Army of Conquest (Jaish al-Fatah) claimed they captured the UN hill in Quneitra and later in the month the Southern Front and Nusra fought in Daraa
  • From Dec 2015-June 2017, there was prolonged fighting between al-Nusra and other Islamist groups against the Free Idlib Army (FSA affiliate) and other divisions within the FSA
  • In March 2016, fighting broke out between Jund al-Aqsa and Ahrar ash-Sham (both Islamist groups) and fighting intensified in Oct 2016 in Idlib
  • From March 2016-May 2016, there was fighting between al-Nusra, Ahrar ash-Sham, and al-Rahman Legion against Jaysh al-Islam in East Ghouta in Rif Dimashq (Damascus)
  • Between Nov 2016-May 2017, there was also infighting in the FSA among the Levant Front and the Sham Legion
  • In Dec 2016-Jan 2017, al-Nusra militants attacked Jaysh al-Islam (Islamist group) in Aleppo and Ahrar al-Sham in Idlib.
  • In Jan-March 2017, there were clashes in Idlib and Aleppo between Ahrar al-Sham against Jabhat Fatah al-Sham (later part of Tahrir al-Sham)
  • In April-May 2017, there was more inter-rebel fighting in East Ghouta between Tahrir al-Sham (a merger of al-Nusra and other Islamic groups) and al-Rahman Legion against Jaysh al-Islam
  • In 2018 and again in 2019, fighting between Tahrir al-Sham and the Syrian Liberation Front (SLF-Islamic group formed in 2018) occured in Idlib and Aleppo.

Jan 2014-2018

Rebel-ISIL Conflict in Syrian Civil War

  • In 2013, the FSA and ISIL cooperated somewhat with only small clashes but then a full-out conflict began to break out in January 2014 when ISIL forces attacked the rebel held town of Atarib in Aleppo
  • In the first week of fighting, the rebels (Islamic Frons, al-Nusra, Ahrar ash-Sham) made large gains against ISIL in Aleppo, Raqqa, Dier ez-Zor, and Aleppo
  • In the second week, ISIL and Islamic Front signed a truce deal in Al-Hasakah and ISIL captured almost all of Raqqa (against al-Nusra) while ISIL and the rebels were in a stalemate in Aleppo
  • During this time, ISIL used suicide bombing attacks, car bombing attacks, and mass executed prisoners they had taken
  • For the rest of the first month, ISIL continued to take some territory but took multiple loses among seniro commanders
  • In February 2014, ISIL attacked the al-Tawhid Brigade (Islamist group) in Aleppo, causing al-Qaeda to break links with ISIL and distance themselves from the group
  • Also in Feb 2014, the Raqqa Revolutionaries Brigade (Islamic group) launched an offensive on ISIL in Raqqa, al-Nusra also launched an offensive on ISIL in Deir ez-Zor and had taken most of the province back within a few days
  • In mid-Feb 2014, the Kurdish Front, the Northern Storm Brigade, and Descendants of Messengers Brigade (FSA affiliated) led an offensive in Northern Aleppo on ISIL
  • In late Feb 2014, ISIL withdrew from the major northern Aleppo city Azaz, with the Northern Storm Brigade and al-Tawhid Brigade taking control
  • In March 2014, ISIL retreated from Idlib and Latakia but took the Al-Hasakah town of Markada in the Battle of Markada

March 2011-present

Syrian Civil War

  • On 12 March 2004, a Syrian Kurdish uprising/riots against the Syrian Arabs took place in the city of Qamishli (al-Hasakah) and culminated in a brutal crackdown/massacre by the Syrian Arab Armed Forces (30 dead and 160 injured) resulting in thousands of Syrian Kurds fleeing to Iraqi Kurdistan
  • In March 2011, anti-government protests broke out as part of the larger Arab Spring and eventually turned into an all out civil war by June 2012
  • [Beligerent 1] Syrian government fighting for Assad: The Syrian Arab Armed Forces with support from Iran, Russia, Hezbollah
  • [Beligerent 2] Syrian Interim Government against Assad: Syrian National Army and Free Syrian Militias supported by a US led coalition
  • [Beligerent 3] Other groups: Syrian Salvation Government (Sunni militants), Autonomous Administration of North and East Syria (Syrian Democratic Forces [SDF], Arab majority led by the Kurdish YPG, supported by US), Hurras al-Din (jihadist al-Qaeda branch), Islamic State (jihadist organization)
  • [Beligerent 4] Turkish forces: Turkish Armed Forces, since 2016 have occupied part of Syria
  • Refugees: 22 million refugees estimated mostly to Turkey, Lebanon, and Jordan but also outside Mid-East such as Germany taking in 500,000 Syrians since 2011
  • Deaths: 470,000-610,000 violent deaths
