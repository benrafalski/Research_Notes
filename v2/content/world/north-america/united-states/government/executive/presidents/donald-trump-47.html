  • Birthright Citizenship On January 20, 2025, Trump signed an executive order to end birthright citizenship in the US. A day after the EO, 22 Dem-led states, the District of Columbia, and the city if San Fransisco sued Trump in Boston and Seattle and the American Civil Liberties Union immigrant organization also filed seperate lawsuits, both of which said Trump's order violated the 14th amendment. [1] Opponents of birthright citizenship say that if the drafters of the 14th amendment wanted all people born in the US to be citizens they would not have said that citizens "be subject to the jurisdiction" of the US. They argue that this language excludes people who entered the country illegally and, by implication, their children. However, most scholars dismiss the interpretation, since people in the country illegally are still subject to the laws of the US and are thus "subject to the jurisdiction" of the US. [2] On January 23, a Seattle US District judge, John Coughenour, blocked the order from being enforces for 14 days while the judge considers a long-term ruling. Coughenour said the order was "blatantly unconstitutional," referring to the 14th amendment that says any person born in the US is a citizen under the Citizenship Clause. [3] On Febrary 6, Coughenour extended his order, setting a nationwide injunction, saying Trump was ignoring the rule of law. [4] Trump later appealed the order but was denied on Feb 19, 2025. [5] On Febrary 5, a second federal judge, U.S. District Judge Deborah Boardman in Maryland, issued a nationwide order blocking Trump from implementing the birthright citizenship EO until the court had ruled on a lawsuit brought to the judge by the CASA and the Asylum Seeker Advocacy Project immigrant rights groups and 5 pregnant women. [6] Trump appealed the Maryland court order but was denied on February 28. [7] On Febrary 10, a third U.S. District Judge, Joseph Laplante, in Concord blocked Trump's birthright EO, saying he expects the issue to be brought to the Supreme Court. [8] On March 13, Trump asked the Supreme Court to narrow the 3 orders against him and that the injunctions should be scaled back from applying universally and should be limited to just the plaintiffs that brought the cases and are "actually within the courts' power." [9] On June 27, the SC ruled 6-3 ordering the 3 lower courts to narrow their nationwide rulings but specified that Trump's EO cannot take effect until 30 days from then. [10] On July 10, Judge Joseph Laplante issued another nationwide injunction blocking Trump's order, despite the Supreme Court's ruling, after the ACLU refiled the case as a class action. [11] On July 23, the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals ruled 2-1 to uphold Judge Coughenour's decision, agreeing that Trump was violating the 14th Amendment. [12] On July 25, U.S. District Judge Leo Sorokin in Boston said his earlier nationwide injuction would remain in place despite the Supreme Court ruling. Sorokin wrote that the evidence before him "does not support a finding that any narrower option would feasibly and adequately protect the plaintiffs from the injuries they have shown they are likely to suffer if the unlawful policy announced in the Executive Order takes effect during the pendency of this lawsuit."   • H-1B Visas In late December 2024, Trump sided with Elon Musk amid a large public dispute over the use of H-1B visas, saying he fully backs the program for foreign tech workers. Musk previously vowed to go to "war" to defend the visa program for foreign tech workers. Trump previously moved to limit the visas' use during his first presidency but seemed to shift on them now. Musk is himself a naturalized U.S. citizen born in South Africa, has held an H-1B visa, and his company Tesla obtained 724 of the visas in 2024. The altercation was set off earlier in the week by far-right activist Laura Loomer who criticized Trump's selection of Sriram Krishnan, an Indian American venture capitalist, to be an adviser on artificial intelligence, saying he would have influence on the Trump administration's immigration policies. Loomer issued an apology later in the week after "accidentally" doxxing Krishnan. [1] [2] On September 19, 2025, issued a proclamation announcing it would start requiring companies to pay $100,000 per year for H-1B worker visas. Microsoft, JPMorgan, and Amazon responded to the announcement by advising employees holding H-1B visas to remain in or to return to the US before midnight on Saturday when the new fee structures are set to take effect. The number of foreign STEM workers in the U.S. more than doubled between 2000 and 2019 to nearly 2.5 million, even as overall STEM employment only increased 44.5% during that time. The move could add millions of dollars in costs for companies, which could hit smaller tech firms and start-ups particularly hard. Analysts suggested the fee could force companies to move high-value work overseas, hampering America's position in the AI race with China. In 2024, India was the largest beneficiary of H-1B visas, accounting for 71% of approved beneficiaries, while China was a distant second at 11.7%. In the first half of 2025, Amazon.com had approval for over 12,000 H-1B visas, while Microsoft and Meta Platforms had over 5,000 H-1B visa approvals each. Despite tech holding a majority of H-1B visas, their CEOs were overwhelmingly quiet about the announcement, likely due to fear of retaliation from Trump. The H-1B program offers 65,000 visas annually to employers bringing in temporary foreign workers in specialized fields, with another 20,000 visas for workers with advanced degrees. Usually, nearly all the visa fees are paid by employers, with the H-1B visas being approved for a period of three to six years. Panic, confusion and anger reigned as workers on H-1B visas from India and China were forced to abandon travel plans and rush back to the U.S. Many criticized the move and the chaotic roll-out with the White House eventually clarifying the hefty fees would be charged just once, not annually, and they would not apply to existing holders including those who happened to be overseas at the time of the announcement. [3] [4] [5] Trump's plan also drew concern from the healthcare industry, with foreign-trained physicians filling 20% of US family medicine posts, disproportionately serving rural and underserved areas. In 2025, of the 442,000 H-1B visas approved, 5,640 were in healthcare. It is also important to note that the US is projected to be short 13,500 to 86,000 physicians by 2036. [8] Due to there only being 85,000 annual visas, the annual allotment is taken up as soon as applications open on April 1st. This lottery system is a bad approach due to higher skilled workers having the same opportunity to get a visa as low skilled workers. The H1-B lasts for three years and can be extended to six, after which workers must either leave the country or transition to a green card. The INA caps the number of green cards to available to applicants from any single country at 7% of the total green cards issued each year, meaning countries with high populations like India and China have really long green card wait times, making their only real route to working in the US being the H1-B visa. [6] [7] According to Pew Research, 57% of H1-B workers had a master's degree and 34% had a bachelor's degree. This means most H-1B workers study at a university and most of them do so in the US, paying higher fees than US-born students in the process, so they can stay after and get a job in the US. With Trump's new fee, this would make so H-1B talent will leave US universities, due to them not being able to get a job in the US after graduating. It also incentivises US firms to hire workers from abroad rather than investing in training domestic talent, which was seen during Trump's first term when H-1B's were under scrutiny. [9] A census report found that 72% of US STEM graduates work in non-STEM occupations. Among the 13.7 million STEM majors working in non-STEM jobs, 36 percent earn more than STEM majors do in STEM jobs. The median STEM major in a STEM job made $110,000 in 2023. Over 4.9 million STEM majors earned at least that much in non-STEM jobs that year. That total is comparable to the 5.1 million STEM majors who work in STEM jobs in total. This means that STEM majors might be choosing to work in non-STEM jobs, or that companies are choosing lower paid H-1B workers over higher paid US STEM workers. [8] Trump administration officials say the visa allows companies to suppress wages, and curbing it opens more jobs for U.S. tech workers. Supporters of the program argue that it brings in highly skilled workers essential to filling talent gaps and keeping firms competitive. In a 2017 NBER study, they found using data from the 1994-2001 tech boom, that there was a substantial rise in foreign high skill workforce from H-1B visas. They found that foreign labor had positive effects on labor and firms earned substantially higher outcome due to the workers. They also found that despite immigration increasing the overall welfare of US natives it also caused wages for US computer scientists to be 2.6% to 5.1% lower and caused employment in computer science for US workers to be 6.1% to 10.8% lower in 2001. On the other hand, immigration also lowered prices and raised the output of IT goods by between 1.9% and 2.5%, benefiting consumers. In a 2016 Berkeley paper, they said "Our primary finding on employment is that additional H-1Bs at most increase total firm employment by a moderate amount. The preponderance of evidence indicates that H-1B workers at least partially crowd out other workers… We find an insignificant effect of additional H-1B visas on patenting and the R&E credit… Overall, our results are more supportive of the narrative in which marginal H-1Bs crowd out other workers, are paid less than alternative workers, and increase the firm's profits—despite little effect on measures of the quantity of firm innovation."" One reason that H1-B workers are paid less is because they are typically younger workers. [6] [7]

    [14342] Taking Steps To End Cashless Bail To Protect Americans August 25, 2025
  • Orders AG Bondi to identify jurisdictions with cashless bail and directs agencies to terminate their federal funding
    		•  Cashless bail is when defendants are released from jail while awaiting trial based on their promise to appear in court, rather than by paying a specific cash amount. Under the policy, a judge assesses whether a defendant should be released from jail based on their risk of not showing up for trial. Critics say it reduces the incentive for defendants to show up for trial and puts public safety at risk by allowing them back on the street. Supporters say many low-income people can't afford to post bond. A 2024 analysis by the Brennan Center for Justice that looked at 22 cities that enacted bail reform, including cashless bail, and 11 that did not between 2015 and 2021 found no evidence of a link to overall crime trends or specific crimes like violent crime and larceny. Cashless bail is a good idea in theory if paired with strong risk assessments and judicial discretion. A blanket release policy is bad, but a structured system that detains the truly dangerous while releasing low-risk defendants is more just and still protects public safety. [1]

  • Moody's cuts US credit: On May 16, 2025, Moody's downgraded the US credit rating due to the rising $36 million US debt. Moody's was the last of the 3 major credit firms to downgrade US credit, the downgraded from Aaa to Aa1. [1]
  • History: Passed House 215-214, passed Senate 51-50 with Vance tie breaker, passed House again 218-214, Trump signed on July 4, 2025. [1]
  • Medicaid: CBO estimated 10 million people on Medicaid will lose coverage due to the bill. The changes include Medicaid eligibility checks twice a year instead of once and 80-hour per month work requirements to receive Medicaid if between ages 19-64. Also replaces the current 6% cap on provider tax revenue (which is collected by states not federally) with a new limit that is indexed to inflation, which indirectly reduces provider taxes. But the bill also sets up a $50 billion rural hospital fund to help rural providers who have argued that the provider tax changes would force them to scale back service. The bill also removes Medicaid eligibility for some non-citizens, including refugees, certain abused spouses and children, and people granted asylum. Also requires states to impose mandatory cost-sharing on expansion enrollees earning more than 100% of the federal poverty level (up from an optional co-pay for above 150% of the poverty level). [1] [2]
  • Border and defense: The bill provides some $350 billion for Trump's border and national security agenda, including $46 billion for the U.S.-Mexico border wall and $45 billion for 100,000 migrant detention facility beds. This includes hiring 10,000 new Immigration and Customs Enforcement officers, with $10,000 signing bonuses. Also gives DHS a new $10 billion fund for grants for states that help with federal immigration enforcement and deportation actions. Also provides billions for ship building, munitions systems, and quality of life measures for servicemen and women, as well as $25 billion for the development of the Golden Dome missile defense system. [1]
  • SNAP: Currently, the federal government fully funds SNAP. Under the bill, states beginning in 2028 will be required to contribute a set percentage of those costs if their payment error rate exceeds 6%. CBO estimates that 3 million people will no longer qualify for SNAP. [1] In September 2025, the BBB SNAP-Ed (low-income school nutrition program) was cut. Since 1992, the USDA has spent $9 billion on SNAP-Ed and drew more than 1.8 million people in 2022. Republican lawmakers argued SNAP-Ed was ineffective and redundant, pointing to a 2019 GAO report that found gaps in how the USDA coordinated and assessed its nutrition program. But the USDA addressed many of those concerns in recent years, according to SNAP-Ed officials. [2]
  • Climate: Ends Biden-era tax breaks on climate issues including on electric vehicles. [1]
  • Tax Cuts and debt: Raised the U.S. government's $36.1 trillion borrowing limit by $5 trillion. CBO says it will add $3.4 trillion to the nation's debt over the next decade. CBO projects it to reduce tax revenues by $4.5 trillion and reduce spending by $1.2 trillion over the next decade. [1] This is because it makes Trump's 2017 tax cuts permanent and expanded tax breaks for parents and businesses, including for tipped income, overtime pay, seniors and auto loans. Nonpartisan analysts have found that the wealthiest Americans will see the biggest benefits from the bill, while lower-income people would effectively see their incomes drop as the safety-net cuts would outweigh their tax cuts. [2] The CBO said the wealthiest households would see a $12,000 increase from the legislation, which would cost the poorest people $1,600 a year. Boosts the $2,000 child tax credit to $2,200. Includes a cap on state and local deductions, called SALT, that will quadruple to $40,000 for five years. [3]
