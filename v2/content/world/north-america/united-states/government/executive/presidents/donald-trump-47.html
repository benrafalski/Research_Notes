  • Birthright Citizenship On January 20, 2025, Trump signed an executive order to end birthright citizenship in the US. A day after the EO, 22 Dem-led states, the District of Columbia, and the city if San Fransisco sued Trump in Boston and Seattle and the American Civil Liberties Union immigrant organization also filed seperate lawsuits, both of which said Trump's order violated the 14th amendment. [1] Opponents of birthright citizenship say that if the drafters of the 14th amendment wanted all people born in the US to be citizens they would not have said that citizens "be subject to the jurisdiction" of the US. They argue that this language excludes people who entered the country illegally and, by implication, their children. However, most scholars dismiss the interpretation, since people in the country illegally are still subject to the laws of the US and are thus "subject to the jurisdiction" of the US. [2] On January 23, a Seattle US District judge, John Coughenour, blocked the order from being enforces for 14 days while the judge considers a long-term ruling. Coughenour said the order was "blatantly unconstitutional," referring to the 14th amendment that says any person born in the US is a citizen under the Citizenship Clause. [3] On Febrary 6, Coughenour extended his order, setting a nationwide injunction, saying Trump was ignoring the rule of law. [4] Trump later appealed the order but was denied on Feb 19, 2025. [5] On Febrary 5, a second federal judge, U.S. District Judge Deborah Boardman in Maryland, issued a nationwide order blocking Trump from implementing the birthright citizenship EO until the court had ruled on a lawsuit brought to the judge by the CASA and the Asylum Seeker Advocacy Project immigrant rights groups and 5 pregnant women. [6] Trump appealed the Maryland court order but was denied on February 28. [7] On Febrary 10, a third U.S. District Judge, Joseph Laplante, in Concord blocked Trump's birthright EO, saying he expects the issue to be brought to the Supreme Court. [8] On March 13, Trump asked the Supreme Court to narrow the 3 orders against him and that the injunctions should be scaled back from applying universally and should be limited to just the plaintiffs that brought the cases and are "actually within the courts' power." [9] On June 27, the SC ruled 6-3 ordering the 3 lower courts to narrow their nationwide rulings but specified that Trump's EO cannot take effect until 30 days from then. [10] On July 10, Judge Joseph Laplante issued another nationwide injunction blocking Trump's order, despite the Supreme Court's ruling, after the ACLU refiled the case as a class action. [11] On July 23, the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals ruled 2-1 to uphold Judge Coughenour's decision, agreeing that Trump was violating the 14th Amendment. [12] On July 25, U.S. District Judge Leo Sorokin in Boston said his earlier nationwide injuction would remain in place despite the Supreme Court ruling. Sorokin wrote that the evidence before him "does not support a finding that any narrower option would feasibly and adequately protect the plaintiffs from the injuries they have shown they are likely to suffer if the unlawful policy announced in the Executive Order takes effect during the pendency of this lawsuit." [13]

  • Immigrants and Welfare Illegal aliens generally do not qualify for federal benefits except for emergency medical care. Under a 1982 Supreme Court ruling, all children regardless of immigration status are entitled to a free K-12 education. On February 19, 2025, Trump's signed executive order "Ending Taxpayer Subsidization of Open Borders" aimed to target a Biden policy of using the parole authority to temporarily allow illegal immigrants into the US including 900,000 from the Mexico border, 500,000 Cubans, Haitians, Nicaraguans and Venezuelans who flew, and 300,000 from Ukraine and Afghanistan. Some undocumented workers obtain jobs by using fake or borrowed Social Security numbers and pay into Social Security, but are not eligible to receive social security benefits under current law. [2] [3] On July 10, Trump expanded the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act of 1996, which bans federal benefits for immigrants from 31 programs, to include 13 new programs, bringing the total of programs to 44. The law did not define what programs count as "federal public benefits," leaving the interpretation up to federal agencies. The newly added banned programs included the Head Start preschool programs for low-income children, various grants or programs for substance use recovery and prevention, the Title X Family Planning Program, health workforce programs, and projects for helping people transition from homelessness. [4] On July 21, 20 Dem states sued Trump over his February EO and on July 25, the Trump admin agreed with them to not go ahead with checking immigration status for services including Head Start, Meals on Wheels, child welfare programs, domestic violence shelters, housing assistance, mental health treatment, food banks, and community health centers. Federal agencies had previously taken the position that states did not have to verify immigration status for some programs, like soup kitchens, homeless shelters, and crisis counseling centers, that received federal funding but were intended to be "open to all."
